Model

Use Prepared Team Member to Model PPE Readiness at Morning Huddle

Before the huddle, identify one team member who will arrive with their additional PPE ready. Call them out by name at the huddle — naming the specific gear and the task. Let the team see what prepared looks like.
The standard
Name the person, the gear, and the task — not just "good job on PPE today"
The callout sets a visible standard for the whole crew, not just recognition for one person
Know who you're calling out before you walk in
What the coach is building
Specificity — "face shield for the grinder" lands harder than "PPE ready"
Using recognition as a standard-setting tool, not just positive feedback
Real world example
Jeffrey A. called out a new employee on his second day for wearing cut-resistant gloves handling raw aluminum. The right person to recognize publicly — new employees are watching what gets noticed.
Morning Huddle Specific Recognition

Ask Team Member to Demonstrate Proper PPE Setup

At a huddle or team moment, ask one team member to physically demonstrate the correct setup for a specific piece of additional PPE — in front of the group. The peer does the demonstrating; the supervisor sets it up.
The standard
Choose someone who knows the setup cold — competence on display, not a test
The group sees correct technique; watching beats hearing about it
Supervisor frames it — team member delivers it
What the coach is building
Moving supervisors away from being the sole source of the standard
Using the team's own expertise to reinforce compliance
Real world example
Greg H. confirmed his team member was comfortable beforehand, ran the demonstration at huddle, and it landed clean. His tip: "Make sure the person is familiar with the process."
Peer-to-Peer Huddle

Stay Protected During Quick Tasks

On a task your team considers quick or low-risk, work alongside a team member with full additional PPE on. Name the gear and the task out loud while doing it. Model that the standard has no "just this once" exception.
The standard
The supervisor gears up too — this is modeling, not instructing
Quick tasks are where the standard erodes first
Credibility: supervisors who model carry more weight when they correct
What the coach is building
Breaking the "it's only a minute" rationalization before it becomes a pattern
Making the standard visible on low-risk tasks, not just high-hazard ones
Real world example
Brent S. worked a material pull alongside a team member — both gloved up together. Instead of a tip on gear storage, he shared personal instances he'd seen go wrong. The team member took it to heart.
Quick Tasks Lead by Example

Correct

Call Out PPE Requirements at Break Transitions

Position at a work area entry as workers return from break. Ask each person to name their required PPE before re-entering. Correct any gaps. Circle back 10–15 min later to visually confirm it held.
The standard
Transitions are where compliance slips most — gear comes off, momentum takes over
Ask before tell: "What do you need before heading back in?"
Loop isn't closed until you've visually confirmed the change held mid-task
What the coach is building
Shifting supervisors from telling to asking — so the team owns the standard
Closing the follow-up loop — most supervisors stop at the correction
Real world example
Donald M. caught a worker returning from break with earplugs around his neck. Before Donald said a word, the worker said "oh shoot, I forgot." Donald followed up twice — both times, they were in.
Break Transitions Ask Before Tell Follow-through Loop

Correct PPE Violations During Operations

During an active shift, walk two work zones looking for PPE violations. Correct violations on the spot. Acknowledge correct compliance in the same pass. Both happen together — not separately.
The standard
Correction and recognition aren't separate activities — they happen in the same pass
Name the specific gear in both cases: "earplugs" not "PPE"
Watch the person comply — don't take their word for it
What the coach is building
Pairing correction with recognition so the standard has two sides
Deliberate walkthroughs — not just catching things incidentally
Real world example
Pat C. spotted someone wearing gloves incorrectly near a saw and someone with an ill-fitting face shield — two zones, two violations, both corrected on the spot. His tip: "Don't be afraid to address someone directly."
Active Shift Recognition + Correction

Verify PPE Feedback Held Throughout Shift

After correcting a PPE gap, return to that team member later in the shift. Look — don't ask. Visually confirm the gear is still on during active work. Acknowledge what you see.
The standard
The follow-up is visual, not verbal — walk by and look, don't ask "are you still wearing it?"
Time the return to when the task is actively happening
The loop closes with acknowledgment, not just confirmation
What the coach is building
Making follow-through a habit, not an afterthought
The distinction between asking and looking — verbal check-ins are easy to game
Real world example
Juan B. said remembering to circle back was the hardest part with production pulling in six directions. His solution: set a phone timer right after the conversation. Followed up twice. Both times, compliant.
Follow-through Loop Shift-long Accountability

Check Team PPE at End-of-Shift

In the final stretch of the shift, walk your team and assess PPE condition — wear, damage, anything needing attention before tomorrow. Recognize what people got right. Correct what needs correcting.
The standard
End of shift is when fatigue-driven shortcuts are most visible
Look at gear condition — not just whether it's being worn
Recognition at end of shift closes the day on the standard, not just the work
What the coach is building
A closing ritual that reinforces the standard daily — not just when something goes wrong
Getting supervisors to inspect gear condition, not just presence
Real world example
Daniel C. spotted a team member tacking without a hood during the shift, ran the end-of-shift check, found gear in working condition. The coach's push: next time, name someone who got it right — close the shift on a positive, not just an audit.

Reinforce

Publicly Acknowledge Consistent PPE Compliance

Identify a team member who consistently uses their additional PPE correctly. At the next huddle, call them out by name, name the specific gear, name the task. Add one line that sets it as the team standard.
The standard
Name the person, the gear, and the task — all three
Close with: "That's what I need to see from everyone"
Consistency is what gets recognized — not a one-time moment
What the coach is building
Recognition as standard-setting, not just reward
Making compliance visible to the whole team, not just the individual
Real world example
Darin S. already had Doug in mind before the coach asked — cut-resistant gloves and face shield for cutting and grinding, every time. Called him out at the 7am huddle. Closed with: "I expect the same from everyone."
Public Recognition Standard-setting

Connect PPE Requirements to Specific Safety Consequences

When explaining or reinforcing a PPE requirement, connect it to a specific injury or near-miss in that work area — not policy, not general risk. Name the body part, the mechanism, and the gear that prevents it.
The standard
Policy they can argue with; a specific injury they can't
"A knife slip goes straight to the tendons — cut gloves are what stands between you and the ER"
Deliver in front of others when possible — the consequence scales beyond one conversation
What the coach is building
Moving supervisors from policy enforcement to consequence framing
"Could get injured" is easy to tune out — a named injury is not
Real world example
Michael W. caught a team member using a knife without gloves — the worker assumed he was in an exempt area. Michael named the laceration risk specifically. The worker thanked him for clearing it up and said he'd do it from now on.
Consequence Framing In the Moment

Acknowledge Good Compliance at End-of-Shift Check

At the end of the shift, identify one or two team members who modeled correct additional PPE use. Acknowledge them specifically — by name, by gear — in front of the team. Close the shift on what was done right.
The standard
Pick specific habits you were watching for — not a general "good job today"
Name the person and the gear: "I watched you keep that face shield on through the whole grind"
This is separate from correction — the shift ends on what was done right
What the coach is building
Making recognition a daily closing ritual, not an occasional event
Watching for compliance specifically — not just violations
Real world example
Curt M. spotted a technician in a face shield during active grinding — called it out directly: "Thank you for wearing the shield and setting a good example." His tip: "I received twenty-four stitches from a grinding accident without a face shield."
