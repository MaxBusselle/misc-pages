Change Happens at Every Level

Sustainable change isn't a one-time event — it's a set of behaviors that have to be built at every level of leadership. This architecture develops those behaviors simultaneously across three audiences, anchored to the ADKAR model your organization is already using.

Three programs. One business problem. Organizational change creates behavioral pressure at every level — and what that pressure looks like differs depending on where you sit. Rather than three disconnected initiatives, this is a single change management architecture deployed across three leadership levels, with ADKAR flowing through each as the shared framework for how people navigate and lead through change.

Program Swim Lanes
Swim Lane 1 · Individual Contributors
Navigating Change
Corporate and office employees who experience organizational change most directly but with the least agency over it. The behavioral failure is going passive — absorbing friction privately, avoiding cross-org relationships, waiting to be told what to do.
ADKAR: Ability · Desire · Knowledge
ICs who complete this program move from passive recipients of change to active navigators — surfacing blockers, building cross-org relationships, and communicating proactively to the people who depend on their work.
Swim Lane 2 · Frontline Leaders
Leadership Communication
New and emerging managers — recently promoted ICs now leading 2–10 direct reports through active organizational change. The behavioral failure is defaulting to IC behavior: doing rather than delegating, directing rather than developing, avoiding difficult conversations.
ADKAR: Knowledge to Ability · Reinforcement
Frontline leaders who complete this program build the foundational habits of effective management — holding people accountable, setting visible standards, and developing their teams through deliberate delegation — at the moment their teams need it most.
Swim Lane 3 · Senior Leadership
Accountability
Directors, GMs, production managers, and functional heads navigating organizational change from the top. The behavioral failure is standards eroding quietly during transition — leaders who champion change verbally but don't model it, whose silence on expectations gets filled by assumption.
ADKAR: Awareness · Knowledge · Reinforcement
Senior leaders who complete this program become the visible anchor of accountability during change — setting clear standards before problems appear, keeping information flowing, and modeling the changes they're asking everyone else to make.
Practices per Swim Lane
Navigating Change · ICs Three practices that make change active, not passive
Practice 1
Surfacing Blockers Early
ICs absorb friction privately rather than naming what's blocking them. This practice builds the habit of raising a specific blocker in a recurring touchpoint before it compounds.
ADKAR: Ability — change stops being theoretical.
Practice 2
Coordinating Across Legacy Teams
During change, people default to familiar relationships and avoid new coordination. This practice builds the habit of proactively coordinating with counterparts across organizational boundaries on shared work.
ADKAR: Desire made behavioral through repeated execution.
Practice 3
Flagging Workflow Changes
ICs are often the first to know when a process changes and the last to tell anyone. This practice builds the habit of communicating changes proactively to whoever depends on your work.
ADKAR: Knowledge moving laterally — the IC becomes a transmitter, not just a recipient.
Leadership Communication · Frontline Leaders Three practices that define effective management from day one
Practice 1
Addressing Performance Issues Promptly
New managers avoid hard conversations or give feedback too vague to act on. This practice builds the habit of naming a specific gap — using the actual work as evidence.
ADKAR: Knowledge becomes Ability.
Practice 2
Reinforcing Performance Expectations
Standards only hold if they're communicated consistently. This practice builds the habit of setting visible targets, following up on commitments, and connecting expectations to why they matter.
ADKAR: the manager becomes the Reinforcement mechanism.
Practice 3
Handing Off Tasks
Strong ICs promoted into management default to doing the work themselves. This practice builds the habit of deliberate delegation — choosing who gets a task and why, setting them up clearly, and debriefing after.
ADKAR: Desire — trust is built through development.
Accountability · Senior Leadership Three practices that hold standards during transition
Practice 1
Setting Clear Standards
Organizational change makes leaders assume their teams know what good looks like. This practice builds the habit of making expectations explicit before problems appear.
ADKAR: the leader signals that standards hold — Awareness.
Practice 2
Communicating Decisions Proactively
Information gaps during periods of change get filled with rumor. This practice builds the habit of sharing what's decided, what's changed, and what's still in motion — before people have to ask.
ADKAR: Knowledge flowing downward.
Practice 3
Modeling the Expected Change
Leaders who champion change verbally but don't visibly live it destroy credibility fast. This practice builds the habit of following through publicly on the specific changes they're asking others to make.
ADKAR: Reinforcement at the organizational level.
Milestones per Practice

Each practice develops across three milestone levels — Basic, Intermediate, and Advanced — representing increasing sophistication and contextual difficulty in how the behavior is executed.

Navigating Change · Practice 1 Surfacing Blockers Early
Basic
Name the Blocker Specifically
Identify one specific obstacle and describe it precisely before raising it — not "things feel uncertain" but a named system, process gap, or unclear role with a real deadline attached.
Intermediate
Raise It in the Right Moment
Surface the blocker in a recurring touchpoint — a standup, check-in, or team sync — rather than sitting on it. Timing matters: raise it while there's still room to act.
Advanced
Propose the Path Forward
Come to the touchpoint with a specific blocker and a suggested next step — not just a problem report. Name what's blocking, what would fix it, and who needs to be involved.
Navigating Change · Practice 2 Coordinating Across Legacy Teams
Basic
Make the First Contact
Identify one counterpart from a different part of the organization and initiate contact on a specific shared task or handoff — a concrete coordination moment, not a general introduction.
Intermediate
Build a Recurring Touchpoint
Establish a recurring check-in or coordination rhythm with a cross-org counterpart — a predictable moment where shared work gets aligned before it creates a problem.
Advanced
Navigate a Cross-Org Conflict
When a coordination breakdown surfaces across organizational boundaries, address it directly — name the tension, surface what each side needs, and work toward a shared resolution.
Navigating Change · Practice 3 Flagging Workflow Changes
Basic
Identify Who Depends on Your Work
Before a process change affects your output, map who downstream depends on what you produce — and confirm they know a change is coming before it surprises them.
Intermediate
Communicate the Change Before It Lands
When your workflow changes, send a specific heads-up to the people who depend on it — what's changing, when, and what they'll need to do differently — before the change takes effect.
Advanced
Close the Loop After the Change
After a workflow change takes effect, check back with downstream colleagues to confirm the transition landed cleanly — surface anything that didn't and fix it before it compounds.
Leadership Communication · Practice 1 Addressing Performance Issues Promptly
Basic
Find the Behavior in the Work
Before delivering feedback, pull a specific recent deliverable and identify exactly what the team member did or didn't do — anchor the observation to a real moment, not a general impression.
Intermediate
Name the Gap and Model the Fix
Use the team member's actual work as the example — identify the specific gap, show what stronger execution would have looked like, and explain why the approach matters.
Advanced
Address a Pattern, Not Just an Instance
When the same gap appears across multiple pieces of work, address the pattern directly — name what's consistently missing, show what good looks like across examples, and set a clear expectation.
Leadership Communication · Practice 2 Reinforcing Performance Expectations
Basic
Set a Visible Weekly Target
State one specific, measurable team target for the week and ask each person to connect their individual goal to it — making expectations visible before the work starts, not after it misses.
Intermediate
Follow Up on a Commitment
When a team member commits to a target or approach, follow up at the agreed time — open with the exact commitment, ask where they stand, and close with a confirmed next step.
Advanced
Connect a Standard to Why It Matters
Explain the business reason behind one of the team's quality standards — what it protects, what breaks when it slips — so the team holds the standard because they understand it, not just because they were told to.
Leadership Communication · Practice 3 Handing Off Tasks
Basic
Assign with a Clear Brief
When delegating, tell the team member what success looks like, what approach to use, and when you'll check in — before they start, not after they've gone in the wrong direction.
Intermediate
Check In and Debrief
Follow the full delegation sequence: check in at a midpoint, let them finish, and debrief after — explaining why the approach you set at the start mattered and what it produced.
Advanced
Match the Task to the Right Person
Before assigning, think through what the task requires and who fits — or who would benefit from the stretch. Tell them why you chose them so the delegation becomes a development moment.
Accountability · Practice 1 Setting Clear Standards
Basic
Name the Standard Before the Gap Appears
In a recurring leadership touchpoint, state one specific performance expectation explicitly — what good looks like, how it's measured, and what you'll do when it slips — before there's a reason to.
Intermediate
Review Results Against the Standard
Pull recent performance data and share one specific result with your leadership team — paired with the standard it's measured against. Ask one question before offering any interpretation.
Advanced
Connect Standards to Organizational Purpose
Explain the business rationale behind one of your BU's operating standards — what it protects at the organizational level and what would break across the integrated company if it slipped.
Accountability · Practice 2 Communicating Decisions Proactively
Basic
Distinguish Decided from In Motion
In any communication to your leadership team, be explicit about what's confirmed, what's still being decided, and what the timeline for clarity is — so people act on facts, not assumptions.
Intermediate
Cascade Before People Have to Ask
When a decision is made that affects your team, communicate it proactively — before questions surface and before rumors fill the gap. Timing is as important as content.
Advanced
Test That the Message Landed
After communicating a significant change, check back with direct reports to confirm the message landed accurately — not "did you get my email" but "what did you take away from that."
Accountability · Practice 3 Modeling the Expected Change
Basic
Name the Change You're Committing To
Identify one specific behavior change you are personally making — not what you're asking of others — and name it explicitly to your direct reports.
Intermediate
Follow Through Visibly
Execute the committed change in a moment where your direct reports can observe it — a leadership meeting, a 1:1, a cross-BU sync. Visibility is the point: your team needs to see it, not just hear about it.
Advanced
Close the Loop With Your Team
After following through, surface the moment to your team — acknowledge what you committed to, describe what you did, and invite their honest read on whether the change is landing.
Next Step: Hooks

Activities only work if they attach to something people already do. Before we build, we need to know the specific recurring moments — meetings, check-ins, reports, handoffs — where each of these practices will naturally live for your people. The questions below are what we need answered, one set per swim lane.

Navigating Change · ICs
Corporate & Office Employees
Recurring Touchpoints
What recurring meetings, standups, or check-ins does this audience attend where status, blockers, or project updates are shared? How often?
Cross-Org Interaction
Are there already recurring touchpoints where employees from different parts of the organization work together on shared tasks or handoffs? What do those look like today?
Work Output
What recurring deliverables or outputs does this audience own — reports, updates, handoffs, data submissions — that others depend on?
Leadership Communication · Frontline Leaders
New & Emerging Managers
1:1s & Team Rhythms
Do new managers hold regular 1:1s with direct reports? How often? Do they run team standups or check-ins — and at what cadence?
Delegation Opportunities
What kinds of tasks or deliverables do new managers typically assign to their teams? Are there recurring assignments that come up weekly?
Performance Visibility
How do new managers currently see their team's work — what deliverables, outputs, or results pass through them on a regular basis?
Accountability · Senior Leadership
Directors, GMs & Functional Heads
Leadership Rhythms
What recurring leadership meetings, ops reviews, or BU performance calls do GMs and Directors run or attend? How often?
Communication Channels
How do senior leaders currently communicate decisions or updates to their teams — all-hands, written updates, cascades through direct reports? What's the existing rhythm?
Change Visibility
What specific changes are senior leaders currently being asked to champion — new systems, restructured processes, or organizational shifts — where their own behavior is most visible to their teams?
Why hooks matter: Every activity in these programs is premised on something your people already do regularly. If the activity doesn't attach to a real recurring moment, it becomes extra work layered on top of existing work — and completion rates suffer. The answers above let us design activities that feel natural, not like training. A 30-minute conversation with someone who knows how these audiences actually spend their week is all we need to move forward.
