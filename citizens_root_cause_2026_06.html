02 OF 03 Session Examples

A closer look at three sessions that show what good root cause work looks like in the field.

These examples illustrate the full diagnostic loop: arriving with a theory, confirming it on site, and leaving with something resolved — not just documented.

Sean Conroy — Senior Facility Manager

Identified recurring temperature complaints at a branch. Before arriving, pulled work order history and noticed tickets had been closed solely based on BMS readings — without verifying actual in-branch conditions. Arrived with a hypothesis: the closure standard was the problem, not the system. On site, confirmed that sensors were miscalibrated and triggering the AC too late. Coordinated with the control center to recalibrate on the spot. Closed the ticket with the actual fix documented.

"Short-sighted fixes don't always lead to long-term solutions. Sometimes it will require the condition to appear more than once to establish a pattern, or to highlight that the fix did not address a root cause — it only solved for the short term."
Sean Conroy, Senior Facility Manager — peer tip
James Wright — Facilities Manager Lead

Pulled up 360 before visiting a branch and found a front door replacement work order open over 60 days. Arrived with the hypothesis that a materials delay was holding it up — not a vendor performance issue. Confirmed on site: doors were on order, no temporary fix was in place, and the branch was using a workaround. Had a maintenance tech adjust the doors for safety in the meantime, called the contractor to get a committed delivery date (end of July), updated the branch manager, regional manager, and supervisor, and logged everything in the work order before leaving.

"Get frequent updates from your contractor or vendor. Be sure to update Retail and keep them in the loop."
James Wright, Facilities Manager Lead — peer tip
Alexander Maxwell — Facility Manager

During a customer restroom walkthrough, noticed a soap dispenser that wasn't dispensing. Opened a work order, then repaired it on the spot. The more interesting finding: the root cause wasn't a broken dispenser — it was a gap in the janitorial inspection checklist. Consumables weren't being checked as part of the routine. Logged both the fix and the process gap before leaving.

"It may not be a major issue — rather a small underlying issue that, through gaps in the process or lack of work order requests, leaves as an ongoing issue."
Alexander Maxwell, Facility Manager — peer tip
