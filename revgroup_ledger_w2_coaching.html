Story of the week
Monitoring PPE During Shifts  ·  Fleetwood — Recreational Vehicles
Pat Curtis
Manager: Randall Baker  ·  Fleetwood
10 activities completed
Pat has gone deeper into this program than anyone — completing activities across all three practices in three weeks. On the floor, Pat identified two PPE violations during a walkthrough (improper glove use near a saw, an ill-fitting face shield) and corrected both on the spot. Pat then recruited a well-respected team member named Paul to demonstrate proper welding sleeve usage at the morning huddle — and recognized him publicly during the demo and privately afterward. Asked what advice to give the next supervisor, Pat's answer was direct: "Find someone well respected. Be enthusiastic. Don't beat around the bush — get right to it."
"Pick someone who's new and help include them in a group setting. They appreciate being called out by name in front of the team."

What the baseline data tells us

The learner baseline (33 complete) and manager baseline (24 complete) give us a clear picture of where this group started. Supervisors rated themselves highest on personal modeling — wearing their own PPE (6.8/10) and observing/correcting violations (5.9/10). These are habits they've built over years on the floor.

The biggest gaps were in recognition and peer modeling — using public acknowledgment consistently (4.7/10), naming specific gear in front of the team (4.8/10), and running peer demonstrations (4.8/10). These are precisely the behaviors the initiative is targeting.

Managers scored their supervisors somewhat higher than supervisors scored themselves — particularly on whether PPE expectations are clear (6.1/10). This modest gap suggests managers may not yet see all the places where standards slip in practice. The initiative is designed to surface exactly those moments.
