⚠️ The Business Challenge

Boyd Gaming operates properties across Nevada and nationwide — ranging from fewer than 50 team members to over 1,300 — each with gaming floors, back-of-house operations, food & beverage facilities, and large-scale mechanical infrastructure. The safety risks are real and the injury costs are measurable: slips, trips, and falls drive workers' compensation claims, ergonomic strains account for significant lost time, and needlestick exposures create both medical costs and regulatory scrutiny. Boyd tracks Total Recordable Case rates by NAICS code, monitors monthly statistics against national industry benchmarks, and leadership watches every dollar spent on workers' comp, clinics, and physical therapy. The challenge isn't that managers haven't completed their annual training — they have. It's what happens after. LOTO audits come back as "I did it" with no documentation. Pre-shift forklift checklists aren't being completed. Department inspections show "no issues" month after month while real hazards — broken floor drains, missing ground pins, unlabeled chemicals — persist. Managers know the rules but aren't actively teaching, coaching, or holding their teams accountable on the floor. When safety issues arise, they come back asking how to have the conversation instead of handling it themselves. This isn't a training gap — it's a leadership practice gap. And closing it is the difference between checking the compliance box and building the kind of safety culture that earns VPP certification.

🏆 The Strategic North Star: VPP Certification

Boyd Gaming's goal is to become the first hotel-casino to achieve OSHA VPP (Voluntary Protection Programs) status — building on current SHARP certification in Nevada. VPP doesn't just require that employees are trained. It requires that managers and supervisors actively lead, coach, and reinforce safety in daily operations. Every activity in this initiative is designed to build the specific leadership behaviors VPP demands — and produce the documented evidence that proves those behaviors are happening.

1
Management Leadership & Employee Involvement — Managers must set clear safety responsibilities, hold themselves and teams accountable, and include safety in performance evaluations
2
Worksite Analysis — Supervisors must conduct regular inspections and observations, investigate incidents and near-misses, and ensure corrective actions are sustained
3
Hazard Prevention & Control — Leaders must coach employees on safe methods, correct unsafe behaviors in real time, and reinforce procedures consistently
4
Safety & Health Training — All employees including managers receive role-specific training; supervisors must know enough to instruct and guide employees on safe work practices

📋 The Documentation Gap

The single biggest obstacle Boyd faces isn't lack of knowledge — it's lack of proof. Properties say they follow safety procedures, but when asked to show evidence, there's nothing there. This initiative addresses that directly: every activity produces documented, verifiable evidence that a manager taught, coached, audited, or corrected a safety practice. No more "I did it" without proof.

❌
LOTO audits requested → "I did it" returned with no supporting documentation, photos, or records
❌
Pre-shift forklift checklists → Not being completed, or completed retroactively at the end of the shift
❌
Department inspections → "No issues found" month after month while broken floor drains, missing ground pins, and unlabeled chemicals persist
❌
Equipment inspections → Microwaves, ground plugs, electrical cords not inspected or documented despite SOP requirements

🛠 Manager & Supervisor Profile

Managers, supervisors, and department leads across all Boyd properties — from 2-manager properties to sites with dozens of leaders
Dual role in this initiative: personally demonstrating correct safety procedures AND teaching their teams to do the same
Responsible for departments with highest safety exposure: Housekeeping (BBP), Facilities (PIT, LOTO, confined spaces), F&B (LOTO, BBP), Warehouse (PIT)
Currently complete annual OSHA compliance training but lack the practice of actively coaching safety on the floor
Tendency to defer difficult safety conversations — coming back to corporate Risk & Safety asking "how do I address this?" instead of handling it directly
Operate in a 24/7 environment where production pressure (keeping machines running, serving guests, short-staffed shifts) competes with safety compliance
Some properties have dedicated safety managers; others have dual-role security/safety personnel — leadership depth varies significantly
SHARP/VPP requirements expect these leaders to go above and beyond annual training — to actively lead, instruct, and guide employees on safe work practices daily
Practice 1

Lock Out / Tag Out (LOTO)

Moving managers from "my team knows LOTO" to "I can prove my team does LOTO correctly, every time, and here's the documentation." This practice builds the ability to conduct and document LOTO audits, identify and map all energy sources on property-specific equipment, and coach team members through correct procedures with verifiable evidence — closing the gap between claimed compliance and proven compliance.

Practice 2

Bloodborne Pathogens (BBP)

Boyd's highest-exposure departments — Housekeeping, Security, Gaming Floor — encounter blood and bodily fluids regularly, and needlestick injuries remain a top cost driver. This practice equips managers to run documented response drills, verify team readiness (PPE stock, kit locations, team knowledge), coach proper exposure response, and produce the evidence that their team can respond correctly — not just that they attended a class.

Practice 3

Powered Industrial Trucks (PIT)

Pre-shift forklift checklists not being completed is one of Traci's most cited documentation failures. This practice tackles it head-on: managers will conduct documented pre-operation inspections, evaluate operators under real working conditions, and coach team members with specific, evidence-backed feedback — building the daily discipline that turns a certification card into genuine operational competence.

Practice 4

Confined Spaces

From grease traps and utility vaults to elevator pits and mechanical rooms, Boyd properties contain permit-required confined spaces that are entered regularly. This practice builds the ability to correctly complete entry permits (not as paperwork, but as life-safety plans), operate atmospheric monitoring equipment with confidence, and lead entry drills with full role rotation — documented at every step for SHARP/VPP evidence.

Operational Context
Where These Practices Come to Life
Activities are written for any Boyd manager, but the safety situations and documentation gaps vary by department. Here are the key operational areas where these practices — and the evidence they produce — matter most.
🎰
Gaming Floor & Slot Operations
Slot Supervisors, Technician Leads
LOTO on slot machines, bill validators, ticket printers — multiple energy sources per machine, high volume of daily entries
BBP exposure from guest injuries at gaming stations — nosebleeds, cuts, medical emergencies on a crowded 24/7 floor
OASIS card and MEAL book procedures intersect with LOTO compliance — machine access documentation already required
Shift handoffs must communicate LOTO status clearly — who locked out what, and when it's safe to re-energize
Where the documentation gap shows up
Technicians skipping LOTO verification on "quick fixes" — no evidence the procedure was followed
BBP cleanup happening without proper PPE or documentation because the spill kit is in the back
Supervisors claiming LOTO audits are done but unable to produce any records when asked
🔧
Facilities & Engineering
Maintenance Supervisors, Engineering Leads
LOTO on HVAC systems, electrical panels, elevators, escalators, boilers — complex multi-energy isolation required
Confined space entry into utility vaults, elevator pits, mechanical rooms, cooling towers — permit-required and high-consequence
PIT operations for equipment transport, renovation support, and materials handling throughout the property
Equipment inspections (ground plugs, electrical cords, power tools) required by SOP but inconsistently documented
Where the documentation gap shows up
Confined space permits treated as paperwork to file, not safety plans to follow — boxes checked without genuine hazard assessment
Atmospheric monitoring skipped because "we've entered this space before and it was fine"
Equipment inspection logs showing "no issues" for months while real hazards persist unaddressed
🍴
Food & Beverage Operations
Kitchen Supervisors, F&B Managers
LOTO on commercial kitchen equipment: mixers, slicers, dishwashers, fryers, walk-in compressors — time pressure is the biggest shortcut driver
BBP exposure from knife cuts, broken glass, burns — fast-paced environment where injuries are frequent and cleanup is improvised
Confined space awareness for grease traps, walk-in cooler/freezer mechanical areas, and exhaust hood ductwork
PIT use in receiving areas where food deliveries intersect with warehouse operations
Where the documentation gap shows up
Reaching into jammed equipment without de-energizing because "service is in 10 minutes" — no documentation trail
Blood cleanup with a wet rag instead of the BBP spill kit — incident never documented
Grease trap cleanouts without atmospheric monitoring or permit documentation
📦
Warehouse & Receiving
Warehouse Leads, Receiving Supervisors
PIT operations are daily and high-frequency — forklifts, pallet jacks, reach trucks in tight spaces with pedestrian traffic
LOTO on conveyor systems, compactors, balers, and dock levelers — often cleared without proper isolation
Mixed traffic zones where delivery drivers, warehouse staff, and property team members share space daily
Loading dock hazards: dock leveler gaps, trailer creep, unsecured loads, and variable lighting conditions
Where the documentation gap shows up
Pre-shift forklift checklists not completed, completed retroactively, or pencil-whipped with identical entries daily
Operators driving without seatbelts or horn use — no one observing and no coaching documented
Baler/compactor jams cleared without LOTO because "it takes too long" — near-misses unreported
🚿
Housekeeping & EVS
Housekeeping Supervisors, EVS Leads
BBP is the primary and highest-frequency exposure risk — blood and bodily fluids in guest rooms, restrooms, gaming areas, public spaces
Sharps hazards: needles and razors found in trash, between cushions, in laundry — needlestick injuries are a top cost driver for Boyd
First-responder role: housekeeping is often the first team to discover a BBP situation and must respond immediately
Chemical handling intersects with BBP cleanup — correct disinfectant selection and contact time are critical
Where the documentation gap shows up
Needlestick or exposure incidents not reported because team members fear discipline or believe "it didn't break the skin"
Sharps picked up by hand instead of with tongs or a sharps container — no training documentation to show proper method was taught
Wrong cleaning products used for BBP situations — supervisors unable to demonstrate their team was trained on correct products
🛑
Security & Emergency Response
Security Supervisors, ERT Leads
BBP first-responder situations: medical emergencies, altercations, guest injuries anywhere on property — often high-stress, time-critical
Scene management that protects the injured person, other team members, and guests from secondary exposure
Coordination with EMS and communication with Surveillance during safety incidents — documented handoff required
Confined space rescue team membership — trained, equipped, and drilled, not just assigned on paper
Where the documentation gap shows up
Responding to medical emergencies without gloves because "there wasn't time" — exposure goes undocumented
Incident reports that capture the event but omit whether BBP protocols were followed during response
Confined space rescue team members assigned but never drilled — no evidence of readiness
