The architecture is in place.

Terex has built a serious HSE infrastructure: a Zero Harm vision, 11 SIF Roadmaps, Safety Leadership Behaviors embedded in performance management, and monthly KPI reporting up to the Executive Leadership Team. What the infrastructure cannot do on its own is develop the on-the-floor behavior that makes it real.

Zero Harm

Terex’s Safety Vision

Zero Harm is not a slogan at Terex. It is the organizing principle of the company’s entire HSE management system — embedded in the Terex Way Values, reviewed by the Board of Directors annually, and tracked through monthly KPI reporting at every level of the organization including the Executive Leadership Team.

The Global HSE Policy defines ten elements of the HSE Management Strategy. Two of them — Management Leadership and Resources & Competencies — are directly about developing the human behavior that makes the other eight work. They are where a program like this lives.

Management Leadership
Safety Leadership Behaviors defined for all levels, evaluated in performance management, integrated into the Leadership Competency model.
Team Member Involvement
Day-to-day HSE activities directed at the site level by operational leaders and HSE experts.

The Gap

What the policy infrastructure cannot do

Policies, roadmaps, and competency frameworks define what the organization expects. They establish the floor. What they do not do is develop the behavior that meets that expectation on the floor, shift by shift. That gap — between the standard and the actual behavior — is where incidents happen and where culture either holds or erodes.

What Roadmaps Do
Define the technical requirement
SIF Roadmaps establish mandatory standards for each hazard category. They specify what compliance looks like and what must be in place. They do not develop the supervisor who enforces them.
What Competency Models Do
Define what good leadership looks like
Safety Leadership Behaviors are defined for all levels and evaluated in performance management. They describe the expectation. They are assessed, not developed — there is no structured mechanism that builds them in the flow of work.
What Remains
Developing the behavior between the assessments
The supervisor who knows the LOTO procedure but does not enforce it. The manager who knows the expectation but does not reinforce it. The VP who communicates Zero Harm but whose team does not feel it as a daily priority. That is the development gap.

Where Flint Fits

In-the-flow-of-work activity practice

The Flint model is structured, on-the-job activity practice: short, specific actions a leader takes on the floor, then reflects on and reports through an AI coaching conversation. No classroom, no pull-off-the-floor training. The development happens in the work itself — which is the only place where safety culture actually forms.

Connects to Management Leadership
Safety Leadership Behaviors are already defined and already in the performance management system. Flint is the development mechanism that builds those behaviors through practice between assessments — not just a tool to evaluate them.
Connects to Resources & Competencies

HSE Leadership Program  ·  Program Design

Building the hierarchy of HSE leadership

This document maps what a structured HSE leadership program looks like when every level of the organization — supervisor through VP/GM — is developing in the same direction, in the same initiative window. Each level plays a different role with the same hazard domain.

The Leadership Model

Three levels. One direction.

Each level has a different relationship to the same HSE domain. The program runs all three tracks simultaneously, so that supervisor behavior change is reinforced from above rather than developed in a vacuum.

Supervisor
Observe, correct, and reinforce on the floor
Closest to the hazard. Activities are immediate and behavioral: catching violations, having the conversation, acknowledging compliance, following up to confirm the change held.
Manager / Director
Acknowledge, reinforce, and model to supervisors
One level removed from the floor. Activities focus on engaging with what supervisors are practicing — surfacing it, removing obstacles, and making it visible across the team.
VP / GM
Communicate HSE as a priority through visible action
Owns the culture. Activities are short, high-signal, and visible to many: sponsoring the initiative, connecting safety performance to business outcomes, making it clear this is not optional.
