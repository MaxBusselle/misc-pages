Section 1

The Initiative

Build PPE Habits with Your Team developed front-line supervisors as active PPE leaders across three practice areas. Each practice targets a distinct behavioral domain — together they form a complete loop from modeling to expectation-setting to sustained monitoring.

Model
Modeling Proper PPE Habits
Supervisors demonstrate the PPE behaviors they expect — wearing the same standards as their team, using prepared team members as examples at morning huddles, and making correct behavior visible in the moment. Modeling removes ambiguity: team members see exactly what compliance looks like before they’re expected to replicate it.

Set Expectations
Setting Clear PPE Expectations
Supervisors make PPE requirements explicit, consistent, and connected to real consequences — not just rules. This covers calling out requirements at transition points, publicly acknowledging compliance, connecting violations to specific hazards, and closing the loop when corrections are made. When expectations are clear, team members know exactly where they stand.
Break transition check-insPublic recognitionConsequence framingFollow-up after corrections
276completed activities
Monitor
Monitoring PPE During Shifts
Supervisors actively observe and respond to PPE behavior throughout the shift — not just at day start. This includes stopping violations during active work, verifying corrections held, acknowledging compliance during observations, and conducting end-of-shift checks for equipment condition. Monitoring closes the gap between intention and execution on the floor.

Section 2

Field Voices

36 quotes from the floor, organized by what kind of moment they capture. These aren’t the best performers — they’re the most specific reporters. Read them as a window into what this program produced on the floor every day for 12 weeks.

The Catch
Supervisors stopping someone mid-task, before harm happens. The moment of intervention — what they said, how the team member responded, and what happened afterward. The correction is the behavior; the follow-up is the habit.
The Ripple
One intervention causing others nearby to self-adjust without being told. A recognition moment that changed the room. The supervisor addressed one person — and the behavior spread.
Consequence Framing
Supervisors connecting PPE requirements to real harm — not the rule, but what happens when the rule is skipped. The moments where the conversation shifted from compliance to understanding.
Equipment Condition
What supervisors found when they looked closely at the gear itself — holes in gloves, scratched lenses, degraded materials. End-of-shift checks created visibility into a risk category that had no structured mechanism before this program.
Honest Partial
Supervisors reporting when they didn’t fully execute — and why. Honest self-assessment is the precondition for improvement. A supervisor who knows they skipped a step is closer to doing it than one who never noticed.
