How to Read This Report

A framework for understanding what we found

Before diving into each PPE category, it helps to understand the lens we're using. Not all compliance gaps look the same — and the distinction matters enormously for what should happen next.

Three Types of PPE Compliance Gaps
Type 1
Habit Gap
The worker knows the rule, has the equipment, and complies when reminded — but the behavior hasn't become automatic. Forgetting is the mechanism, not resistance. Most common at break transitions.
Type 2
Knowledge Gap
The worker is missing a specific piece of information — about which PPE is required for a task, what worn equipment has lost its protective value, or what the actual risk of exposure is. Requires education, not just correction.
Type 3
System Gap
The environment is creating barriers — PPE doesn't fit correctly, the right type isn't stocked, equipment degrades faster than replacement schedules account for. Cannot be solved by manager coaching alone.

The data across all four PPE categories reveals a mix of all three types. Understanding which type is driving a given violation is the starting point for designing an effective response.

01
Eye Protection / Safety Glasses
Highest mention volume across all field entries — 169 total across violation, correction, and coaching data
Highest Frequency

Eye protection is the most consistently present but most inconsistently worn PPE category in the data. The equipment is almost always available. The behavioral failure is in how and when it's used.

🔍
The core finding: glasses are present but not protecting

The dominant pattern is not workers without glasses — it's workers whose glasses are lifted onto their forehead, pushed up on their hat brim, or temporarily removed during tasks. To a passing observer, the worker looks compliant. From a safety standpoint, the eyes are unprotected.

Behavior Drivers by Sub-Type
  • Forehead/hat: habit gap — glasses are lifted briefly for clarity and not replaced. Automatic behavior that bypasses conscious decision-making.
  • Wrong type: knowledge gap — worker doesn't know standard glasses are insufficient for overhead work, grinding, or under-vehicle tasks
  • Prescription gap: knowledge gap — workers may not know side-shield programs exist or that their Rx glasses don't qualify as safety eyewear
  • Scratched lenses: system gap — no self-reporting mechanism; workers wear degraded equipment because replacement isn't prompted
Highest-Risk Contexts
  • Returning from break or lunch — glasses left in break room, not re-equipped before floor re-entry
  • Quick tasks — glasses lifted to see detail on a small component and not reset before continuing adjacent work
  • Overhead and under-vehicle work — standard glasses inadequate; goggles or gasketed glasses required but not worn
  • Morning start-up gatherings — some managers note glasses are removed during huddles even when on an active production floor
What Consequence Framing Looks Like for Eye Protection

The most effective manager-reported approaches connect the removal of glasses to a concrete, imaginable outcome rather than restating the rule. Several approaches from the field:

"I shared an experience from years prior when I witnessed a worker remove his safety glasses while troubleshooting an air system on one of the fire trucks. At that moment, the air system suddenly blew out a blockage — mud dauber debris had built up inside. The force of the release covered his face with debris and bug remains, and he narrowly avoided serious eye injury."

Floor Manager — on consequence framing for eye protection
📌
Key operational note: The prescription glasses gap is a systemic issue with a known fix — a safety Rx eyewear program. The data suggests workers are unaware the program exists. This is a communication and access problem, not a compliance problem.
02
Gloves / Hand Protection
134 mentions across all field entries — second highest volume, and the #1 category in equipment condition findings
High Frequency

Glove violations present across two distinct contexts: active use failures (not wearing gloves during tasks that require them) and equipment condition failures (wearing gloves that have degraded past their protective value). Both are prevalent, and they have different root causes.

⚠️
The core finding: two separate problems masquerading as one

The "not wearing gloves" problem is primarily a habit gap at transition moments. The "worn-out gloves" problem is a knowledge and system gap — workers prefer broken-in gloves and don't know degraded cut resistance is invisible. These require different interventions.

The Worn Gloves Problem in Detail
  • Workers prefer broken-in gloves — they feel more comfortable and fit better. This is not defiance; it's a logical preference based on an incomplete understanding of how cut resistance degrades
  • Degradation is invisible — a glove can look merely "worn" while having lost significant protective value. Workers have no mechanism to recognize the threshold
  • Loctite 243 is an accelerant — departments using this chemical have a shorter effective glove lifespan that existing procurement schedules may not account for
  • Replacement is available on demand — multiple managers report replacing gloves on the spot, but workers aren't self-initiating replacement because they don't recognize the need
The Active Use Problem in Detail
  • "Just a quick grab" — the most cited context for not wearing gloves is a brief material handling moment where putting gloves on feels disproportionate to the duration of the task
  • Wrong type for the chemical — cut-resistant gloves used during solvent or chemical work; chemical-resistant gloves needed but not recognized as a distinct requirement
  • After restroom or break — gloves set down, not re-equipped before returning to material handling tasks. Mirrors the eye protection pattern exactly
  • Gloves hanging from belt — workers have gloves but aren't wearing them. Access is not the issue; the habit of wearing them during all material contact is.

"I noticed that Loctite 243 will cause the protective side of the gloves to deteriorate and fall apart. My department uses Loctite 243 quite frequently — which means gloves will have to be replaced more frequently."

Floor Manager — identifying a procurement-level implication from field observation
📌
A notable manager behavior: Multiple managers independently distributed glove clips — a physical holder that keeps gloves on the belt — as a solution to the "set down and forgot" pattern. This is a creative system-level fix invented at the floor level, not instructed. It signals that the habit gap has a potential environmental design solution.
03
Hearing Protection
73 mentions — third-ranked category, with a strongly concentrated failure pattern
Medium Frequency

Hearing protection violations are less frequent than eye or glove failures, but they carry a distinct behavioral profile: unlike other PPE categories, hearing violations are almost never about defiance or comfort preference. They are almost entirely about forgetting.

👂
The core finding: a pure habit gap, concentrated at one moment

The data does not show workers who refuse to wear ear protection, who find it too uncomfortable, or who argue against the requirement. It shows workers who remove ear protection during breaks and lunches — a natural and expected behavior — and fail to re-insert before returning to noise-exposure areas. The failure is entirely at the re-entry point.

The hidden violation: One manager, during a morning huddle, noticed a team member who appeared fully compliant — but had only one ear plug in. The other had fallen out without the worker noticing. This violation is categorically invisible unless a manager is within arm's length. It may be more prevalent than the data captures.
Why Consequence Framing Is Especially Effective Here

Hearing loss is uniquely suited to future-consequence framing because it is permanent, irreversible, cumulative, and entirely silent until it's too late. Several managers independently discovered that framing ear protection as a decision about their hearing decades from now — not about today's rule — produced the most durable behavior change they observed.

"Ear plugs and the negative consequences of hearing loss in years to come without wearing them. She acknowledged me being right and put them in after the discussion."

Floor Manager — on consequence framing for hearing protection
What Makes Hearing Different
  • No immediate pain or visible signal — unlike a cut or a bump, there is no sensory feedback from noise exposure in the moment
  • Damage is cumulative — missing ear protection for a single shift feels inconsequential; the risk calculus is spread over years
  • Workers know they should but don't feel an urgency — connecting "today's break" to "lifelong hearing loss" is the critical bridge managers need to build
Manager Self-Disclosure Pattern
  • At least one manager explicitly reported removing his own ear plugs during video training to hear better — then reflected that he should find a private area instead
  • This is a meaningful data point: managers are holding themselves to the same standard they're applying to their teams, and disclosing when they fall short
  • Manager credibility on hearing protection depends partly on their own visible compliance in hearing-protection zones
04
Face Shield & Bump Cap
64 mentions — the most task-specific PPE category; violations are concentrated in two specific work contexts
Emerging Focus

Face shields and bump caps are unlike the other categories: violations are not spread across all work contexts. They cluster with striking consistency in two specific situations — under-vehicle work and grinding operations. The violation is not forgetting; it's judgment that standard PPE is sufficient for what's perceived as a "quick" task.

🪖
The core finding: a task-specific knowledge gap, not a general habit failure

Workers wearing their standard safety glasses and general PPE are often unaware that overhead, under-vehicle, or grinding work requires additional protection. The logic is intuitive but incomplete: "I have my safety glasses on — why do I need a face shield too?" The answer requires connecting task-specific hazards to task-specific PPE requirements.

The "Quick Task" Logic Gap
  • Workers frequently justify skipping a face shield or bump cap by assessing task duration — "just a quick tack," "I'll only be under there a second"
  • This logic is a misunderstanding of how hazards work — arc flash exposure, debris from grinding, and head impacts do not correlate with task duration
  • One manager's response to this: "He said he was only trying to save time. It was explained to him that his safety is more valuable than the few seconds it takes to put his hood on."
Equipment Condition — Face Shields Specifically
  • Face shields found heavily scratched and scuffed — reducing visibility significantly while technically still in use as PPE
  • This creates a secondary hazard: the worker's field of vision is impaired, increasing risk of other incidents
  • One manager's note: "The face shield designated for grinding operations was found to be extremely scuffed and worn, reducing visibility through the shield"

"I told him that saving a few seconds by skipping the face shield isn't worth a permanent eye injury. Even if it's just a quick grind on the chassis, it only takes one stray spark to change everything."

Floor Manager — on the quick-task logic trap
Cross-Cutting Analysis

Three patterns that run across every category

Stepping back from the four PPE categories, three structural patterns appear in the data that cut across all of them. These are not category-specific findings — they are behavioral and systemic patterns that any effective Phase 2 response will need to account for.

Pattern 1

The break transition is the single highest-risk moment across all PPE categories

Eye protection, hearing protection, and gloves all show the same spike: the transition from break room to production floor is the moment where PPE compliance drops most reliably. Workers remove equipment for breaks — a natural, appropriate behavior — and the re-equipping step is not yet habituated. This is not unique to any one type of PPE; it is a structural gap in the transition routine itself.

💡
The implication: Solving this pattern requires targeting the transition moment itself — not individual PPE types in isolation. A threshold-to-threshold reinforcement approach, or a physical cue built into the transition space, would address all three categories simultaneously.
Pattern 2

Corrections hold — the behavioral change that happens after a manager intervenes tends to last

Of 52 documented follow-up checks, 33 confirmed that the corrected behavior was still in place later in the shift — and in many cases into subsequent shifts. This is a meaningful finding: the manager intervention is working. Workers are not reverting as soon as the manager turns away. This suggests the challenge is not stubbornness or resistance — it is the initial habit gap that needs addressing, and managers are successfully addressing it one interaction at a time.

Pattern 3

Equipment condition is a system problem, not a compliance problem — and it's currently invisible without a check structure

Across gloves, face shields, safety glasses, and respiratory masks, the data shows damaged or degraded PPE in active use — not because workers are ignoring the issue, but because there is no routine mechanism for workers to identify when their equipment has crossed the protective threshold. End-of-shift checks initiated through this program are the only reason these findings exist. Before the initiative created that check structure, this gap was entirely hidden.
