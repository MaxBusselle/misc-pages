1. Probate vs. Non-Probate Property
Probate Property ▼
  • Property that does not transfer automatically to another person or by a trust at death
  • Title is in the decedent's name
  • Includes tenancy-in-common interests
  • No one can take title without court involvement (probate process)
💡 It is easier to name what is non-probate; everything else is probate estate.
Non-Probate Property / Will Substitutes ▼

Passes automatically without court involvement, by operation of law, by contract, or by terms of a trust:

  • Joint Tenancy (WROS) – e.g., real estate, bank accounts
  • Tenancy by the Entirety – CA NOT available in CA; only for spouses
  • Beneficiary Designation / POD Arrangements – owner designates beneficiary; transfer done by company holding asset (e.g., retirement accounts, IRAs, POD accounts)
  • Revocable "Transfer on Death" Deeds – owner puts beneficiary on deed; available in CA → repeal in 2026
  • Revocable (Living) Trusts – inter vivos; created by owner while living
  • Trusts – a relationship, not an entity; trustee = legal title; beneficiary = equitable title

⭐ 2025 Federal Estate Tax Exemption: $13.99 million ▼
The 2025 federal estate tax exemption is $13.99 million. The executor is responsible for paying decedent's debts and taxes, then distributing assets to beneficiaries.
Probate Process — Steps ▼
  1. Determine jurisdiction (based on domicile)
  2. Determine venue
  3. Find last Will (if any)
  4. Lodge the Will (original filed with County Clerk; anyone can lodge)
  5. File petition to appoint executor/administrator and "admit" the Will for probate
  6. Hearing on Petition for Probate
    • Notice given to all heirs, named persons, CA AG (if charitable foundation named)
    • Notice of death published in local newspaper
    • Letters Testamentary issued to executor (or Letters of Administration to administrator)
  7. Examine if ancillary probate is needed
CA Venue = county where decedent was domiciled at time of death (not where they died)

Alternatives to Probate (California) ▼
  • Affidavit procedure – estates up to $184,500 of probate assets (no real property) can be settled by affidavit. Increasing to $750,000 effective 4/1/25, and can include primary residence.

3. Will Formation & Execution
⭐ General Formalities (Majority Rule) ▼
  • Testator must be an adult of sound mind
  • Must be IN WRITING
  • SIGNED by the testator (a mark is OK if intended as signature; someone else may sign if testator unable)
  • Witnessed by two individuals — witnesses and testator sign in each other's presence
  • All pages must be present when Will is signed (physically connected)
  • Dating is not required but encouraged
  • Testamentary intent: "This is my Will," "I bequeath/devise/leave"
CA Prob. Code § 6110 Witnesses must sign DURING the testator's lifetime. Post-death witnessing is NOT allowed in California.

CA Prob. Code § 6112 Will is NOT invalid because signed by interested witness. Creates a rebuttable presumption of undue influence if fewer than 2 other disinterested witnesses exist. Interested witness may only take up to their intestate share if presumption applies.
Integration, Incorporation by Reference, Facts of Independent Significance ▼
  • Integration: All pages of Will intended to be part of it must be present when signed; should be physically connected
  • Incorporation by Reference: Most jurisdictions accept (NOT NY, CT, LA). Document must be (1) in writing, (2) exist at date of signing, (3) identifiable with reasonable certainty. Need not be present at signing.
  • Facts of Independent Significance: Allows identification of gifts by reference to other facts (e.g., "jewelry in my safe deposit box")
  • Separate Writing for Tangibles (UPC § 2-513): Separate statement for tangible personal property (NOT money, stocks, or real estate). Can be updated after Will signed. MUST be signed.
CA Prob. Code § 6132 Max value $25k; max $3k per item. NO CASH OR COIN. Tangible property not otherwise disposed of in a Will.
A document created AFTER a Will is signed is effective as part of the Will under neither integration nor incorporation by reference.
4. Will Validity — Capacity & Defects
⭐ Testator Capacity — Low Standard ▼

Lower than capacity to contract. Must be:

  1. An adult (at least 18)
  2. Of sound mind — checked at time Will was executed

Restatement Third: testator must be capable of knowing (1) nature and extent of property, (2) natural objects of bounty, (3) disposition being made, and (4) relating these elements to one another to form an orderly desire.

An Alzheimer's diagnosis does NOT automatically render someone incapacitated. Wilson v. Lane.
A person is presumed to have capacity until proven otherwise. There is no such thing as a "presumption of lack of capacity."
Insane Delusion ▼
  • Belief against all evidence and reason that must be product of derangement (mere eccentricity is NOT insane delusion)
  • Elements: (1) belief of facts with no real existence, (2) false belief is product of mental illness, (3) no evidence supporting false belief, (4) delusion causes a disposition testator would not otherwise make
Consequences: Only the PART of the Will affected by the delusion is void. Void gift(s) fall to residue or intestacy. Balance of Will is valid.
Compare to lack of capacity: lack of capacity invalidates ENTIRE Will; insane delusion only invalidates the affected part.
⭐ Undue Influence ▼

Wrongdoer overcame donor's free will, causing donative transfer donor would not otherwise make. "Regular" influence is OK.

Elements (Restatement § 8.3):

  1. Testator was susceptible
  2. Alleged influencer had opportunity
  3. Alleged influencer had disposition to exert undue influence
  4. Part or all of Will appears to be the result

Presumption arises if: (1) confidential relationship exists AND (2) suspicious circumstances (e.g., they helped draft the Will)

Types of confidential relationships: Fiduciary (attorney/client, trustee/beneficiary — matter of law); Reliant (doctor/patient — matter of fact); Dominant-Subservient (caregivers — matter of fact)

CA Prob. Code §§ 810–812, 21380 Gifts to drafting attorneys, caregivers, partners, or relatives are VOID unless fiduciary proves by clear and convincing evidence not the product of undue influence. Requires a Certificate of Independent Review by independent attorney.
Fraud — Types & Consequences ▼
TypeConsequence
Fraud in the inducementOnly the part of the Will affected is invalid; rest is valid (testator intended to sign a Will)
Fraud in the executionEntire Will is invalid (testator did not intend to sign a Will)
Fraud in preventing revocationWill will not be probated
Constructive trusts available for fraud in the inducement and preventing revocation — NOT for fraud in the execution.
Curative Doctrines: Saving a Will ▼
  • Reformation: Correct mistakes — clear and convincing evidence required
  • Substantial Compliance: Close enough is good enough — (1) C&C evidence of testamentary intent AND (2) Will substantially complies with formalities
  • Harmless Error (UPC § 2-503): Court may excuse noncompliance if C&C evidence testator intended document to be Will
  • Dependent Relative Revocation (DRR): Revocation not actually a revocation if conditional on a 2nd Will that was never executed or was flawed; reinstates original bequests
  • Revival: Revocation of Will #2 may revive Will #1 depending on intent
CA Harmless Error Can overcome requirements for 2-witness presence, witness signing during testator's lifetime, and witness understanding document is Will. Requires C&C evidence of intent.
Holographic Wills ▼
  • In testator's own handwriting; recognized by ~half the states including CA (NOT NY)
  • UPC § 5-502(b): Signature and material provisions must be in testator's handwriting; preprinted forms are fine if material portion is handwritten
CA Requirements: Signed by testator (anywhere); material provisions (gifts made, beneficiaries' names) in testator's own handwriting. Statement of testamentary intent not required (but extrinsic evidence admissible). Date not required. Preprinted forms allowed.
Revocation — Methods & Rules ▼

Formalities strictly enforced for revocation, just like execution.

Methods:

  1. Physical act with intent to revoke (burning, tearing, canceling, obliterating, destroying)
  2. New Will that is inconsistent with previous Will (or expressly states revocation)
If Will #2 doesn't dispose of entire estate → presumption of intent NOT to revoke Will #1 (rebuttable by C&C evidence). If Will #2 disposes of entire estate → presumption to revoke Will #1 (rebuttable).
Lost Wills: If Will was in testator's possession until death and is lost, presumed destroyed with intent to revoke. Someone else should keep the original Will.
5. Construction of Wills — Gifts & Lapse
⭐ Types of Gifts ▼
  • Specific Devise: Gift of a specifically identifiable asset (e.g., "my house," "my Rolex watch")
  • General Devise: Gift payable from general assets of the estate (e.g., "$X cash")
  • Demonstrative Devise: Gift primarily payable from a stated source, secondarily from general assets (e.g., "$100,000 payable from my Chase Bank account")
  • Residuary Devise: Gift of property not disposed of by the above gifts
⭐ Abatement — Order When Estate Insufficient ▼

Priority: payment of funeral expenses, attorney's fees, and taxes FIRST. Then Will controls; if silent, statute applies:

  1. Intestate assets abate proportionately (if any)
  2. Residue abates proportionately among multiple residuary devises
  3. General bequests abate proportionately
  4. Specific bequests abate proportionately
Specific gifts are the SAFEST. They are the last to abate.
Ademption — Specific Devises Only ▼

Occurs when an item gifted in a Will is no longer part of the testator's estate → gift fails ("adeemed"). Applies ONLY to specific devises.

  • Identity Rule (majority): If asset not in estate, NO GIFT. Exception: "mere change in form" (mink coat → stole; bank acquired by another bank)
  • UPC § 2-606(a) (Non-ademption/Substitute Gifts): Unpaid proceeds, insurance proceeds, replacement property pass to devisee
⭐ Anti-Lapse Statutes ▼

Prevents lapsed gifts (to predeceased legatee who is a relative) from passing by intestacy. Passes to deceased legatee's descendants.

  • Based on presumed intent of testator
  • If testator's clear intent to make gift conditional, gift lapses
  • For class gifts: if anti-lapse doesn't apply, surviving class members take
UPC Words of survivorship ("if he survives me") are NOT sufficient evidence of contrary intent. Must be more specific to prevent anti-lapse.
Majority Rule: "if he survives me" DOES evidence intent not to substitute descendants. Wilma takes Blackacre.
Lapsed Gift SituationTraditional RuleModern/UPC/CA
Lapse in residuary clause (2+ people)Passes by intestacy (no-residue-of-a-residue rule)Other residuary devisees get 100%
Anti-lapse for spousesMajority does NOT apply to testator's spouse—
Divorce & Killers ▼

Divorce: Even if Will never changed, divorced spouses do NOT inherit. Divorce MUST be FINAL (legal separation is not enough).

MajorityUPC & CA
ScopeWills onlyWills, non-probate transfers, joint tenancy, beneficiary designations, POAs, nominations as executor/trustee
Former spouse's relativesNoUPC includes revocation of gifts to former spouse's relatives
CA Person who feloniously and intentionally kills testator does NOT inherit — not under Will, trust, or non-probate transfers. Joint ownership deemed severed. NO anti-lapse for killer's kids. Need: final criminal conviction OR civil court finding by preponderance.
Omitted Children & Spouses ▼

Omitted Child: Child born after testator signed ALL Wills and codicils. There is NO RIGHT TO INHERIT (except Louisiana).

TraditionalModern/UPCRevised UPC (& CA)
ShareIntestate shareIntestate share, or same as other childrenGets intestate share unless: omission appears intentional; provided for outside Will; substantially all estate left to other parent

Omitted Spouse: Married after testator signed all estate plan documents. Entitled to: (1) deceased spouse's half of community/quasi-community property; (2) intestate share of separate property (½ if only 1 child, ⅓ if 2+ children) but no more than 50%.

⭐ CA Intestacy — Non-Spouse Priority Order ▼
  1. Issue (equally at 1st generation with living issue, then by right of representation)
  2. Parents (equally, or all to surviving parent)
  3. Issue of Parents (in same manner as decedent's issue)
  4. Grandparents (equally, or all to surviving grandparent)
  5. Issue of Grandparents
  6. Issue of predeceased spouse
  7. Next of Kin
  8. Parents of Predeceased Spouse
  9. Issue of Parents of Predeceased Spouse
  10. Escheat to State of California
120 Hour Rule: Any heir must survive by 120 hours (5 days), otherwise deemed predeceased — unless escheat would occur. Applies to non-probate transfers as well.

7. Gifts
⭐ Inter Vivos Gift — Elements ▼
  1. Donative Intent: Present intent to transfer without consideration; surrender dominion and control. Goodwill NOT required.
  2. Delivery: Must occur during donor's lifetime.
    • Manual: Physical transfer
    • Constructive: Delivering what controls the item (key to safe)
    • Symbolic: May reserve rights (deed to real property)
  3. Acceptance: Usually presumed if gift has value
Checks: Complete when presented to donor's bank for payment (revocable until cashed). Mailbox Rule: Duly-executed deed mailed to donee is complete upon placing in mailbox.
Gifts Causa Mortis: Gift in contemplation of death. Fail if donor recovers or donee predeceases donor. Revocable. Subject to creditors. Limited to personal property. Must be delivered.

BeneficiaryEquitable title; benefited per trust terms; "watchdog" of trustee

⭐ Types of Trusts — Payment Distributions ▼
TypeDescriptionCreditors
Mandatory TrustAll income must be paid to A for life, remainder to B. Trustee has NO discretion.Easier for creditors to reach
Discretionary TrustDistributions made as needed per standard (health, support, education). Trustee has discretion.Creditors cannot reach before trustee decides to distribute

CA Spendthrift Exception: Only child support (not spousal support). UPC: child support, spousal support, attorney providing services to protect beneficiary's interest.
⭐ Trustee Duties ▼
  • Duty of Loyalty: Administer solely in interests of beneficiary. No self-dealing (buying/selling to/from trust). No further inquiry rule — transaction voidable without regard to fairness. Cannot commingle assets.
  • Duty of Impartiality: Act impartially between current and remainder beneficiaries (not necessarily equal, but with due regard)
  • Prudent Investor Rule: Manage as a prudent investor; investments viewed in context of whole portfolio; diversification required
  • Duty to Collect & Protect: Without unreasonable delay
  • Duty to Earmark: Title assets appropriately in trustee capacity; keeps assets out of reach of trustee's personal creditors
  • Duty to Inform and Report: Keep beneficiaries reasonably informed; within 60 days of accepting trusteeship, notify beneficiaries
  • Duty to Account: Annual report of trust property, liabilities, receipts, disbursements
Exculpatory Clauses (UTC § 1008(b)): Cannot waive bad faith or recklessness. Drafted-by-trustee clauses are invalid unless trustee proves it is fair and contents were adequately communicated to settlor.
Charitable Trusts & Cy Pres ▼

Charitable purpose: Must benefit the public at large. Merely benevolent is NOT charitable. Purposes include: relief of poverty, advancement of education, advancement of religion, promotion of health, governmental/municipal purposes, other community-beneficial purposes.

  • No limit on duration (not subject to RAP)
  • Beneficiaries need not be ascertainable

Cy Pres Doctrine: Modifies a trust so settlor's intentions are carried out "as near as possible." Invoked when original terms are impossible, impracticable, or illegal. Only allowed when donor had GENERAL charitable intent.

Enforcement: State AG has authority. Under UTC § 405 (majority): donor has standing while living; AG; individual current beneficiaries.

Modifying / Terminating Trusts ▼
SituationRule
Settlor + ALL beneficiaries consentCan modify/terminate without court approval (UTC)
Settlor + some beneficiaries consentCourt may modify/terminate if non-consenting beneficiary's interests are protected
No settlor consent — "Material Purpose" test (Claflin)All beneficiaries must consent; court may terminate if continuance not necessary to achieve material purpose
A spendthrift clause is NOT a material purpose under the UTC.
9. Powers of Appointment
⭐ Powers of Appointment — Key Concepts ▼

Power held by a person to designate recipients of beneficial ownership interests. Non-fiduciary — no duties or obligations, only restrictions stated in the document.

TypeGeneral PowerSpecial/Non-General Power
BeneficiariesDonee may appoint to themselves, creditors, estate, or othersCannot benefit: powerholder, creditors, estate, or creditors of estate
Permissible appointeesNot required to be identifiedMUST identify permissible appointees
Estate taxIncluded in powerholder's taxable estateNot included
Creditors (created by another)Presently exercisable: subject to creditors if other property insufficientGenerally not reachable by creditor

Key vocabulary: Donor (confers power); Donee/Powerholder (holds power); Objects/Appointees (permissible recipients); Takers in Default (take if power not exercised)

10. Future Interests & RAP
⭐ Rule Against Perpetuities (RAP) ▼
"An interest must vest, if at all, no later than 21 years after lives in being at the creation of the interest."
  • Look at MOST REMOTE POSSIBILITY — if ANY fact pattern could violate RAP, it is violated
  • Modern trend: 90-year "wait and see" rule
  • Indefeasibly vested remainder → NOT subject to RAP
  • Remainder vested subject to open → SUBJECT TO RAP
  • Contingent remainder → SUBJECT TO RAP
  • Reversionary interests → NOT subject to RAP
  • Vested interests → NOT subject to RAP (can last longer than RAP period)
Interest TypeSubject to RAP?
Indefeasibly vested remainderNO
Vested remainder subject to openYES (until class closes)
Contingent remainderYES
Reversion (interest retained by transferor)NO
Life estate (possessory)NO — not a future interest
