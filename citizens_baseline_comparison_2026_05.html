What the numbers show: The cohort is nearly split — slightly more overconfidence than underconfidence, but both directions are well represented. The near-zero group average gap can be misleading; it masks the fact that nearly half the cohort has meaningful divergence between how they see themselves and how their manager sees them. These gaps are the most valuable part of the baseline.
Question-Level Analysis
What the Group Rated by Dimension
The two surveys asked different but related questions. Learner questions (5) map to self-perception of habits and behaviors. Manager questions (3) map to observable, from-the-outside behaviors. Direct question-to-question comparison isn't possible, but the pattern across dimensions tells a story.
Key pattern: Learners rate their intent and mindset (fresh eyes mindset, customer experience lens) higher than their documented output (360 documentation fidelity — the lowest self-rated dimension at 5.8). Managers independently rate documentation specificity as their lowest dimension (5.8). Both surveys point to the same gap: the doing is ahead of the recording.

Manager Calibration
How Each Manager Scored Their Team
Managers rated their direct reports on 3 behaviors (1–10 scale). The range across managers is striking — from 3.0 to 9.3. Several managers gave all of their direct reports identical scores across all three questions, suggesting some may be rating their team as a unit rather than differentiating individuals. This calibration context is essential for interpreting individual gaps.
Calibration note: 6 of 11 managers rated all their reports at a completely uniform average — meaning every person on their team got the same score across every question. John Scaglia's entire team was rated 3.0 uniform. Shawn Evans rated all 8 of his reports at 8.0 uniform. Maria Kingsley rated all 3 of hers at 9.0 uniform. When you see large gaps for individuals reporting to these managers, consider the manager's overall scale calibration before drawing conclusions about the individual.
