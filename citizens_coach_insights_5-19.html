01 / 03 Coach Focus Areas

What the coach is spending most of its time on

Across 62 completed activities, three coaching themes emerged with enough consistency to tell us something real about where the learning is happening.

69%
of activities involved coaching around the pre-entry reset habit — starting from the parking lot, not the work order
60%
of activities required coaching on documentation specificity — what it is, where exactly, how long it's been there
37%
of activities required the coach to redirect participants from exterior-only to the full interior customer route
Theme 1 — The Reset Habit
FMs are arriving at branches and going straight to the work order
The coach's most consistent focus has been on the moment of arrival — specifically the habit of stopping at the parking lot and mentally resetting into a customer lens before doing anything else. This is the hardest behavior to interrupt because the FM's pull toward their operational purpose is strong.
"The reset moment is everything. Start in the parking lot and work your way in as a customer would."
Coach, to Vincent Parrino
Theme 2 — Documentation Quality
The gap isn't noticing things — it's documenting them specifically enough to act on
Most participants are finding issues. The coach's recurring challenge has been translating what they see into a 360 entry that's useful. The most common gap: participants log a category ("cleanliness issue") rather than a finding ("cove base separating at teller counter, ~6 feet, not on any open work order").
"Always document, even if the information was communicated outside the work order system."
Michael Konczewski, FM — peer tip to colleagues

02 / 03 Participant–Coach Dynamics

How participants are engaging — and where the friction is

The coach interactions reveal a consistent pattern: participants have the right instincts, but hit predictable friction points when translating intent into action.

What's working
Participants are finding real things
76% of completed activities resulted in a confirmed 360 entry. The most common customer-visible findings: door frames and hardware (42 activities), ceiling tiles (32), paint condition (16), and landscaping (10).
"One tip I would give the FM is to not hold back — just because you may know what's going on, all things need documentation."
Taeyon Thompson, FM
Where friction shows up
The interior walk is being skipped
In 37% of activities, participants completed an exterior walkthrough but didn't make it through the full interior customer route — entrance, lobby, teller line, restrooms. Landscape-focused FMs were especially likely to stay outside.
"I focused primarily on the outside."
David Schofield, FM — after completing exterior-only walk
"Next time, even 5 minutes inside before you leave makes a big difference."
Coach response
Patterns by role
Experienced FMs show a different friction point
Senior FMs and Facilities Leads are more likely to find the issue and fix it on the spot — without logging it. Their instinct is to resolve, not document. Newer FMs are more likely to need coaching on what to look for.
"The MT completed the work already… good that it got handled fast! But going forward, still log it in 360 even if it's already fixed."
Coach, to Michael Konczewski (SFM)
A pattern worth watching Three participants cited photo friction specifically — file size limits on mobile cameras and 360 upload issues. This may be suppressing completion quality in ways the data alone won't show.

03 / 03 What the Coach Is Teaching Us

What this tells us about your workforce

The coaching patterns aren't just about activity completion — they're revealing something about how this team thinks about their role and their relationship to the customer experience.

What the data suggests
The customer lens isn't absent — it's competing
Participants self-rated their customer-mindset awareness at an average of 6.8 out of 10 at baseline. That's not low. But the coach interactions reveal that when participants arrive at a branch, the operational pull is stronger than the customer lens. It's not that they don't care — it's that the FM brain takes over the moment they walk in. The habit the initiative is building is specifically about interrupting that default.

The tension the coach is navigating
Fix it now vs. document it first
One of the most consistent dynamics across the coaching conversations is experienced FMs resolving issues on the spot and skipping the documentation step. This reflects real competence — they know how to handle problems. The coaching challenge is reframing documentation not as paperwork, but as the mechanism that makes the pattern visible across hundreds of locations.
"For a guy with your experience, that habit of fixing things immediately is natural — but the fresh-eyes technique is specifically about capturing what you see before you act on it, so there's a record and a pattern over time."
Coach, to James Wright (FML, 20 years experience)
