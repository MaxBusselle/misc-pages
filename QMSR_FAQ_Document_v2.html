QMSR COMPLIANCE FAQ

For ISO 13485 Certified Medical Device Manufacturers

Effective Date: February 2, 2026

Reference: 21 CFR Part 820 (QMSR) | ISO 13485:2016 | CP 7382.850

Updated February 2026 to incorporate FDA Compliance Program CP 7382.850

ABOUT THIS DOCUMENT

Audience: Quality professionals at ISO 13485:2016 certified medical device manufacturers

Purpose: Clarify what QMSR changes, what stays the same, and practical implications for compliance

Sources: QMSR Final Rule (89 FR 7496, Feb 2, 2024), ISO 13485:2016, ISO 9000:2015, CP 7382.850 (Jan 30, 2026)

FAQ 1

What does QMSR actually change versus what stays the same?

KEY TAKEAWAY

The requirements are substantially similar. The real shift is not in what you must do, but in how compliance is evaluated, what records are now accessible to FDA, and the enforcement framework — which moved from QSIT’s checklist-based subsystem model to a risk-based inspection process organized around six QMS Areas and four OAFRs under CP 7382.850.

FDA’s own characterization: “substantially similar” requirements

• FDA states this rulemaking “does not fundamentally alter the requirements for a QS that exist in either the former QS regulation or the new QMSR” (Comments 17–18, pages 7504–7505)

• The concepts and requirements of the old QS Regulation, “when viewed holistically, are contained in ISO 13485”

• This is not a massive overhaul of what you must do — it is a restructuring of how compliance is framed and enforced

What specifically IS new or changed:

• Record-keeping: Enhanced record-keeping requirements in § 820.35 (complaint handling records) and § 820.45 (device labeling and packaging controls)

• Inspection access: The § 820.180(c) exemption that protected internal audit records, management review records, and supplier audit records from routine FDA inspection has been removed (Comment 55, pages 7513–7515)

• Terminology: QMSR adopts ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3 vocabulary by reference — terms like “customer,” “nonconformity,” and “verification” now use ISO definitions unless FDA has explicitly redefined them in § 820.3 (Comment 26)

• Risk-based approach: ISO 13485 more explicitly integrates risk-based thinking across Clauses 4.1, 7.1, 7.3, 7.4, 7.5, 7.6, and 8.2 (Comment 19, pages 7504–7505)

• Inspection framework: FDA has retired QSIT and published CP 7382.850 (January 30, 2026), replacing the four-subsystem checklist approach with a risk-based inspection process organized around six QMS Areas and four OAFRs (CP 7382.850, Part III, page 20)

What stays the same:

• Scope: applies to finished device manufacturers, not component makers or third-party servicers

• FDA’s legal inspection authority under Section 704 of the FD&C Act

• Core QMS expectations: design controls, production controls, CAPA, complaint handling, document control

• How combination product CGMP requirements work under 21 CFR Part 4

• ISO 13485 certificates do not replace FDA inspections; FDA inspections do not issue ISO certificates

BOTTOM LINE

For ISO-certified companies, the gap is not primarily in new requirements — it is in new enforcement exposure. What was previously shielded from FDA view is now visible, the inspection framework has shifted from checklists to risk-based process tracing, and the enforcement lens has moved from “did you document it” to “can you prove people actually do it.”

Key Citations: Comments 17–18 (pp. 7504–7505); Comment 19 (pp. 7504–7505); Comment 26 (p. 7506); Comment 55 (pp. 7513–7515); CP 7382.850 Part III (p. 20); FD&C Act § 704

FAQ 2

What do I specifically need to do to be QMSR compliant if I am already ISO 13485 certified?

KEY TAKEAWAY

Your ISO 13485 certification covers the vast majority of QMSR requirements. Your gap analysis should focus on FDA-specific additions, terminology alignment, new record accessibility, and preparing for the risk-based inspection process defined in CP 7382.850 — not just the ISO standard.

Step 1: Start with a targeted gap analysis, not a full rebuild

• QMSR incorporates ISO 13485:2016 by reference — your existing certified system is the foundation

• Focus your analysis on the specific areas where FDA adds to, amends, or supplements ISO 13485

• Review your QMS documentation to ensure it references the correct regulatory framework (21 CFR Part 820 as amended, not just ISO 13485)

Step 2: Check for FDA-specific additions on top of ISO 13485

• Complaint handling: § 820.35 — complaint handling records with specific investigation, documentation, and UDI requirements

• Labeling/Packaging: § 820.45 — specific U.S. requirements for device labeling and packaging controls

• UDI requirements: Part 830 — UDI documentation requirements that go beyond ISO 13485 scope

• Definitions: QMSR adds definitions in § 820.3 that differ from or supplement ISO 9000/ISO 13485 definitions where U.S. law requires it

Step 3: Align vocabulary with ISO 9000:2015 Clause 3

• QMSR incorporates ISO 9000:2015 vocabulary by reference (Comment 26)

• Terms like “customer,” “supplier,” “nonconformity,” and “verification” must be understood as defined in ISO 9000 unless QMSR § 820.3 provides a different definition

• Note: this is ISO 9000 (vocabulary), not ISO 9001 (requirements) — only the definitions section applies

Step 4: Prepare for the new enforcement exposure

• Review internal audit records, management review minutes, and supplier audit reports for investigation-readiness

• Ensure these records demonstrate substantive engagement, not just checkbox compliance

• ISO certification alone will not satisfy FDA (Comment 80: “FDA disagrees with the comments that recommend the Agency accept certification to ISO 13485 in place of FDA inspections”)

Step 5: Prepare for the new inspection structure

• Study CP 7382.850’s six QMS Areas and four OAFRs — this is how investigators will walk through your system (CP 7382.850, Attachment A, pages 70–78)

• Verify your QMS covers all applicable requirements, including those outside ISO 13485 (Parts 803 MDR, 806 corrections/removals, 821 tracking, 830 UDI)

• Ensure your system can be navigated by ISO 13485 clause references, not just legacy QS Regulation paragraph numbers

BOTTOM LINE

Your ISO certification is a strong starting point, not a finish line. The practical work is in reviewing FDA-specific supplements, preparing internal records for potential inspection, and ensuring your system can be demonstrated through the lens of CP 7382.850’s risk-based inspection framework.

Key Citations: Comment 26 (p. 7506); Comment 80 (pp. 7517–7518); § 820.3; § 820.35; § 820.45; 21 CFR Parts 803, 806, 821, 830; CP 7382.850 Attachment A (pp. 70–78)

FAQ 3

How will FDA inspections and investigations be different under QMSR?

KEY TAKEAWAY

FDA published CP 7382.850 on January 30, 2026 — a 78-page Compliance Program Manual that replaces QSIT and fundamentally restructures how inspections are planned, scoped, executed, and escalated. The framework shifts from QSIT’s four subsystems to six QMS Areas and four OAFRs, driven by product risk to patients and users. Internal audit, management review, and supplier audit records are now fully accessible.

The new inspection framework: CP 7382.850

• Published January 30, 2026, effective February 2, 2026 (CP 7382.850, Cover Page)

• Supersedes CP 7382.845 and CP 7383.001 (PMA pre/postmarket inspections) — consolidating everything into a single compliance program

• 78-page operating manual that tells investigators exactly how to plan, scope, execute, and escalate inspections under QMSR

From QSIT subsystems to QMS Areas and OAFRs (CP 7382.850, Part III, pages 20–24)

• QMS Area 1: Management Oversight — QMS planning, risk-based approach, management commitment, management review, human resources, planning of product realization

• QMS Area 2: Design and Development — full design lifecycle from customer processes through design files

• QMS Area 3: Production and Service Provision — infrastructure, work environment, process validation, identification and traceability

• QMS Area 4: Outsourcing and Purchasing — supplier controls, purchasing processes, purchased product verification

• QMS Area 5: Change Control — QMS changes, software changes, product and process changes, purchasing changes

• QMS Area 6: Measurement, Analysis, and Improvement — feedback, complaint handling, internal audits, CAPA, nonconforming product, data analysis

• OAFR 1: Medical Device Reporting (21 CFR 803)

• OAFR 2: Reports of Corrections and Removals (21 CFR 806)

• OAFR 3: Medical Device Tracking Requirements (21 CFR 821)

• OAFR 4: Unique Device Identification (21 CFR 830)

Two inspection models (CP 7382.850, Figures 1–3, pages 23–25)

• Model 1 (risk-selected): For non-baseline surveillance, compliance follow-up, for-cause, SPRA, and PMA postmarket inspections. Investigators select at least one element from each of the six QMS Areas and evaluate each of the four OAFRs. Selection is driven by identified product risks.

• Model 2 (prescriptive): For baseline surveillance (no prior FDA inspection or MDSAP audit history) and PMA preapproval inspections. Specifies particular elements within each QMS Area that must be evaluated — minimum 22 elements for non-sterile devices, 23 for sterile. More prescriptive and comprehensive.

The critical record access change (Comment 55 + CP 7382.850)

• Under the old QS Regulation, § 820.180(c) exempted management review, internal audit, and supplier audit records from routine FDA inspection

• Under QMSR, this exemption is explicitly removed — “FDA disagrees that it should maintain the exceptions set forth at § 820.180(c)” (Comment 55)

• CP 7382.850 operationalizes this: Management Review is an element within the Management Oversight QMS Area; Internal Audits are an element within Measurement, Analysis, and Improvement (Attachment A, pages 73, 75)

Key operational changes

• Risk management documentation is the inspection roadmap — investigators review it throughout the inspection to decide which areas to prioritize and which records to sample (CP 7382.850, Part III.1.B, page 21)

• Investigators expand scope if objectionable conditions are found or if evaluating one requirement necessitates evaluating requirements in other areas — creating a direct feedback loop (page 21)

• Under FDARA, delaying, denying, limiting, or refusing an inspection — including refusing to provide requested documentation — can cause a device to be deemed adulterated (CP 7382.850, Part I.1, page 7)

MDSAP considerations

• MDSAP-participating firms remain exempt from routine surveillance inspections (CP 7382.850, pages 26–28)

• However, they remain subject to for-cause, compliance follow-up, SPRA, and PMA-related inspections

• FDA continues to review and classify MDSAP audit reports; concerns raised by that review can trigger an FDA inspection

BOTTOM LINE

CP 7382.850 is the most transparent look FDA has ever given industry at how it plans to inspect. Read it. The framework is risk-driven, record-access barriers are gone, and investigators will follow risk signals through your quality system rather than checking boxes in a fixed sequence.

Key Citations: CP 7382.850 (Jan 30, 2026): Cover Page, Part I.1 (p. 7), Part III.1 (pp. 20–24), Figures 1–3 (pp. 23–25), Attachment A (pp. 70–78); Comment 55 (pp. 7513–7515); FDARA § 301(k); FD&C Act §§ 510(h), 704

FAQ 4

What process will the FDA inspectors follow under QMSR?

KEY TAKEAWAY

FDA published the answer: CP 7382.850 details a risk-based inspection process where investigators use your risk management documentation as the roadmap, select elements within six QMS Areas based on product risk to patients, and can move flexibly across areas as findings dictate. There are no fixed sampling tables — record selection is driven by risk and investigator judgment.

The inspection process step by step (CP 7382.850, Part III.1.B, pages 20–22)

• Step 1 — Preparation: Investigators become familiar with the manufacturer’s roles, products, and processes to understand what requirements are applicable

• Step 2 — Risk identification: Investigators identify product risks that could adversely impact patients and/or users, reviewing registration and listing, marketing authorizations, previous 483 observations, recalls, MDR data, and the inspection assignment

• Step 3 — Risk documentation review: Investigators review the manufacturer’s risk management documentation throughout the inspection to understand product risks and associated risk controls

• Step 4 — Element selection: Based on identified risks, the investigator selects elements within QMS Areas and OAFRs to evaluate. QMS Areas are not required to be evaluated in any specific order.

• Step 5 — Requirement evaluation: The investigator evaluates related requirements within selected elements, reviewing multiple records to provide assurance

• Step 6 — Scope expansion: If objectionable conditions are found, or if evaluating one requirement necessitates evaluating others, investigators expand scope into additional elements and areas

No sampling tables — a significant change from QSIT

• CP 7382.850 contains no sampling tables and does not direct investigators on how many records to review

• Investigators select records based on identified product risks and professional judgment

• “Multiple records should be reviewed to provide assurance” — but specifics are left to the investigator

• This is more flexible for FDA and less predictable for manufacturers

Risk management failures as OAI triggers (CP 7382.850, Part V.1.A, pages 50–51)

• Situation 1 examples: Failure to establish, implement, or maintain one or more processes for risk management in product realization

• Failure to use feedback or postmarket surveillance as inputs into risk management

• Failure to control design and development, including not adequately evaluating changes for risk and impact on products

• Failure to adequately analyze data or utilize current risk information, resulting in decisions not to proceed with investigations or corrective actions, leading to unmitigated adverse health consequences

• These findings can result from underestimated risk, outdated risk information, or inadequate risk management used to make decisions

What your team must be ready for

• Investigators who move across QMS Areas following risk threads, not a fixed checklist sequence

• Requests for risk management documentation early in the inspection — this is their roadmap

• Questions about how risk-based decisions were made, with documented rationale, not just outcomes

• Potential requests for internal audit reports, management review records, and supplier audit documentation — refusal can trigger adulteration under FDARA

• Scope expansion if early findings reveal problems — a weak area triggers broader coverage

BOTTOM LINE

The inspection framework is no longer a mystery. CP 7382.850 is publicly available and tells you exactly how investigators will approach your facility. Organizations that study it, prepare their risk management documentation as the inspection entry point, and can demonstrate compliance across the six QMS Areas will be best positioned.

FAQ 5

Will the FDA evaluate “culture of quality” differently now that the ISO 13485 standard is legally required?

KEY TAKEAWAY

Yes. FDA defined “culture of quality” in the QMSR Preamble as meeting regulatory requirements through “behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes.” CP 7382.850 carries this language directly into the inspection framework, citing it in a footnote on page 20 and making Management Oversight a full QMS Area with elements that evaluate whether leadership is actually driving quality.

FDA’s definition — now embedded in the inspection framework

• Comment 27 (pages 7506–7507): “FDA expects medical device manufacturers, led by individuals with executive responsibilities, to embrace a culture of quality as a key component in ensuring the manufacture of safe and effective medical devices.”

• “A culture of quality meets regulatory requirements through a set of behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes.”

• CP 7382.850 quotes this language directly in footnote 10 (page 20): “FDA expects top management to ensure applicable regulatory requirements are met through integrating QMS processes and embracing a culture of quality.”

Management Oversight as a full QMS Area (CP 7382.850, Attachment A, page 73)

• Management Oversight is one of the six QMS Areas — not a background check but a core inspection area

• Elements include: Management Commitment (Clause 5.1), Quality Policy and Objectives (Clauses 5.3, 5.4), Management Review (Clauses 5.6.1–5.6.3), Human Resources (Clause 6.2), and Risk-based Approach (Clause 4.1.2)

• Under both inspection models, investigators must evaluate at least one element from Management Oversight — and under Model 2, Management Review is a required element

Why this matters more under QMSR than under QSR

• Under QSR: management review and internal audit records were protected from FDA inspection via § 820.180(c)

• Under QMSR + CP 7382.850: these records are now accessible, meaning FDA can see whether leadership engagement is substantive or performative

• CP 7382.850 explicitly instructs investigators to evaluate whether management reviews consist of boilerplate vs. active decision-making — disconnects between review records and actual complaint/CAPA trends will be visible

What FDA investigators will look for in management review records

• Evidence of: Clear inputs: quality data, CAPA/complaint signals, supplier performance, audit results

• Decision-making: Explicit priorities, resource assignments, and risk trade-offs with documented rationale

• Follow-through: Assigned owners, due dates, and evidence of completion and effectiveness

• Compliance factor: CP 7382.850 Part V.1.B (page 53) lists “Manufacturer’s Commitments” as a compliance factor: evidence of “commitment from top management” and “allocation of adequate resources”

BOTTOM LINE

Culture of quality is no longer aspirational — it has regulatory teeth. FDA defined it in the Preamble, embedded it in the inspection framework via CP 7382.850, and made the records that demonstrate (or fail to demonstrate) leadership engagement accessible to investigators.

Key Citations: Comment 27 (pp. 7506–7507); CP 7382.850 Part III.1.A footnote 10 (p. 20), Attachment A Management Oversight (p. 73), Part V.1.B (p. 53); ISO 13485 Clauses 5.1, 5.6

FAQ 6

Does this mean that the FDA inspectors are doing the same thing as my ISO 13485 auditors?

KEY TAKEAWAY

No. ISO 13485 auditors verify conformity to a voluntary international standard. FDA investigators verify compliance with U.S. law. CP 7382.850 makes the structural differences even more explicit: FDA inspections evaluate six QMS Areas plus four OAFRs (MDR, corrections/removals, tracking, UDI) that extend well beyond ISO 13485’s scope.

The core distinction

• ISO 13485 auditors assess your QMS against the standard’s requirements

• FDA investigators verify compliance with the FD&C Act and all implementing regulations — including not only QMSR but also Parts 803 (MDR), 806 (corrections/removals), 821 (tracking), and 830 (UDI)

• CP 7382.850 formalizes this by requiring investigators to evaluate four OAFRs in addition to the six QMS Areas — these OAFRs have no equivalent in ISO 13485 audits (Attachment A, page 77)

FDA’s explicit position (Comments 79–80, pages 7517–7518)

• “FDA does not intend to require medical device manufacturers to obtain ISO 13485 certification”

• “An ISO 13485 certificate will not be considered or accepted as a substitute for any oversight processes, including the performance of an inspection under section 704 of the FD&C Act”

• “FDA inspections will not result in the issuance of a certificate of conformity to ISO 13485”

BOTTOM LINE

Your ISO auditor and your FDA investigator are looking at overlapping but fundamentally different things, through different legal lenses, with different consequences. CP 7382.850’s OAFRs make this especially clear: MDR compliance, UDI, corrections and removals, and tracking have no ISO 13485 equivalent.

FAQ 7

What happens if the FDA finds discrepancies between my stated ISO 13485 policies and procedures, and actual practices?

KEY TAKEAWAY

The “say-do gap” has always been an inspection risk. Under QMSR + CP 7382.850, the risk compounds: FDA can now see your internal audit records documenting the very gaps they discover on the floor, investigators are instructed to expand scope when findings reveal objectionable conditions, and repeat violations trigger a specific “recidivist policy” with escalating enforcement.

The compounding exposure under QMSR

• An FDA investigator finds that your SOP says one thing and your production floor does another

• Previously, your internal audit records — which may have documented this same gap — were shielded under § 820.180(c)

• Under QMSR, the investigator can now request those internal audit records

• If your own records show you knew about the discrepancy, you move from “unaware noncompliance” to “documented awareness without adequate corrective action”

CP 7382.850’s scope expansion mechanism (Part III.1.B, page 21)

• Investigators “should consider evaluating additional requirements, as applicable, if an inspection reveals objectionable conditions or if evaluation of one requirement necessitates the evaluation of requirements in other areas”

• This creates a direct feedback loop: one weak area triggers broader inspection coverage across additional QMS Areas

• A say-do gap found in production can lead the investigator into management review, CAPA, internal audit, and training records

The enforcement continuum (CP 7382.850, Part V, pages 50–54)

• OAI triggers: Situation 1 — Serious, systemic, and/or repeat deviations with evidence of adverse impact or significant risk to patients/users → OAI classification and potential regulatory action

• VAI findings: Situation 2 — Less significant deviations with minimal or no public health impact → VAI classification; Form 483 informs the establishment

• Key factor: At each level, evidence that you were already aware of the issue (via your own audit records) strengthens FDA’s position

The recidivist policy (CP 7382.850, Part V.3.D, page 59)

• FDA has a specific enforcement strategy for manufacturers with repeated violative inspections

• Recidivist Warning Letters require the manufacturer to submit annual certifications by an outside expert consultant confirming a complete QMS audit

• The CEO must personally certify receipt, review, and completion of all corrections and corrective actions

• FDA notes this pattern “is often due to the failure of the organization to effectively implement and maintain a quality management system”

The corrective action dimension

• If your CAPA records show the gap was identified, a root cause was determined, and corrective actions were implemented and verified for effectiveness — this is a positive signal

• If your audit records show the gap was identified but corrective actions were not completed, not effective, or not documented — this is a Situation 1 finding: “Failure to adequately correct the same or similar significant deficiencies from previous inspections” (Part V.1.A.(1)(f), page 51)

• Even documented struggles represent positive evidence if they demonstrate systematic identification and response

BOTTOM LINE

Under QMSR + CP 7382.850, the say-do gap is no longer just an inspection risk — it is an evidence chain that FDA can trace through your own records. The best protection is not hiding problems but demonstrating systematic identification, response, and resolution. Repeat failures trigger a specific escalation pathway.

Key Citations: Comment 55 (pp. 7513–7515); CP 7382.850: Part III.1.B (p. 21), Part V.1.A Situation 1 (pp. 50–51), Part V.3.D Recidivist Policy (p. 59); ISO 13485 Clauses 8.2.4, 8.5.2, 8.5.3

FAQ 8

What are these 83 industry “comments” I keep hearing about?

KEY TAKEAWAY

The 83 comments are FDA’s organized responses to public input on the proposed QMSR rule, published in the Preamble of the Final Rule. They are the closest thing to FDA “showing their work.” CP 7382.850 frequently cites these comments, making them the interpretive foundation for the new inspection framework.

What the Preamble is

• The QMSR Final Rule was published in the Federal Register on February 2, 2024 (Vol. 89, No. 23, pages 7496–7530)

• Before the rule was finalized, FDA published a proposed rule and invited public comments from manufacturers, trade associations, auditing organizations, and other stakeholders

• The Preamble to the Final Rule contains FDA’s organized responses to these comments, numbered Comment 1 through Comment 83

How they are organized

• Section A: General Comments on Proposed Rule (Comments 1–16)

• Section B: Scope (Comments 2–7)

• Section C: Incorporation by Reference (Comments 8–15)

• Section D: Definitions (Comments 16–32)

• Section E: Requirements for a QMS, Clarification of Concepts, and Supplementary Provisions (Comments 33–77)

• Section F: Implementation Strategy — inspections, MDSAP, certification (Comments 78–83)

The connection to CP 7382.850

• CP 7382.850 directly cites the Preamble comments as interpretive authority — for example, footnote 10 (page 20) quotes Comment 27 on culture of quality

• The compliance program references FDA’s response to Comment 19 on risk management as the basis for its risk-based inspection approach (footnote 5, page 8)

• Read them together: the Preamble explains what FDA intends; CP 7382.850 operationalizes how investigators will evaluate it

The most consequential comments

• Comment 19: Risk management — FDA’s expectations for risk-based thinking throughout the full product lifecycle, now operationalized as OAI triggers in CP 7382.850

• Comment 27: Culture of quality — FDA’s definition as “behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes,” now quoted in the inspection framework

• Comment 55: Removal of § 820.180(c) exemptions — internal audit, management review, and supplier audit records now accessible to FDA, operationalized through Management Oversight and MA&I QMS Areas

• Comments 78–81: Inspection approach, MDSAP alignment, and FDA’s position on ISO certification — now concretely answered by CP 7382.850

BOTTOM LINE

Read the Preamble and CP 7382.850 as companion documents. The Preamble explains what FDA intends and why. CP 7382.850 tells investigators how to evaluate it. Together, they form the most complete picture available of what QMSR compliance looks like in practice.

Key Citations: Federal Register Vol. 89, No. 23 (Feb 2, 2024), pp. 7496–7530; CP 7382.850 footnotes 5 (p. 8), 10 (p. 20); Comments 1–83 organized by topic

FAQ 9

What internal audit documents, previously confidential under ISO 13485, are now fair game for QMSR inspections?

KEY TAKEAWAY

Important nuance: these records were never “confidential under ISO 13485.” The protection came from the old QS Regulation at § 820.180(c) and the QSIT guide. Under QMSR, that protection is explicitly removed. CP 7382.850 operationalizes this by placing Internal Audits and Management Review as inspectable elements. And under FDARA, refusing to provide requested documentation can cause a device to be deemed adulterated.

Clarifying the source of the old protection

• The old QS Regulation § 820.180(c) specifically exempted management review records, internal audit reports, and supplier audit reports from routine FDA inspection

• The QSIT inspection guide reinforced this by directing investigators not to routinely request these records

• ISO 13485 itself never contained such an exemption — other regulators worldwide have always had access to these records

Where these records now sit in the inspection framework (CP 7382.850, Attachment A)

• Internal Audits — an element within the Measurement, Analysis, and Improvement QMS Area (page 75), referencing ISO 13485 Clause 8.2.4

• Management Review — an element within the Management Oversight QMS Area (page 73), referencing Clauses 5.6.1, 5.6.2, 5.6.3

• Outsourcing (which includes supplier audits) — an element within the Outsourcing and Purchasing QMS Area (page 76), referencing Clause 4.1.5

• Under Model 2 (baseline/PMA preapproval), Internal Audits and Management Review are required elements that must be evaluated (Figure 2, page 24)

Specific record categories now accessible to FDA

• Internal audit plans, schedules, scope, and criteria

• Internal audit findings, conclusions, and corrective actions

• Management review inputs (quality data, CAPA, complaints, audit results, supplier performance)

• Management review outputs (decisions, action items, resource allocation, improvement plans)

• Supplier audit reports and supplier qualification records

• Gap assessments and improvement plans

The FDARA dimension — refusing documentation carries consequences (CP 7382.850, Part I.1, page 7)

• FDARA amended the FD&C Act so that a device is “deemed to be adulterated” if the establishment “delays, denies, or limits an FDA inspection or refuses to permit entry or inspection”

• This includes refusing to provide requested documentation

• An inadvertent refusal based on outdated assumptions about what FDA can and cannot see could create a serious compliance problem

• Every person who may interact with an FDA investigator needs to understand that the § 820.180(c) protection no longer exists

How to write internal records in this new environment

• Write internal audit findings as though they will be read by an FDA investigator — because they now can be

• Document findings factually and objectively, with clear corrective action plans and timelines

• Ensure management review records demonstrate active leadership engagement, not just meeting attendance

• A finding with documented corrective action is evidence of a healthy system; a finding without follow-through is evidence of a broken one

BOTTOM LINE

The protection is gone, and refusing to produce these records carries legal consequences under FDARA. These are no longer internal-only documents — they are potential investigation evidence. Write them with the same care and precision you would apply to any externally-facing compliance documentation.

Key Citations: Comment 55 (pp. 7513–7515); CP 7382.850: Part I.1 FDARA (p. 7), Attachment A — MA&I (p. 75), Management Oversight (p. 73), Outsourcing and Purchasing (p. 76), Figure 2 (p. 24); FDARA § 301(k)

FAQ 10

How does QMSR treat risk management differently than the old QS Regulation?

KEY TAKEAWAY

Risk management under QMSR is not a standalone activity — it is the operating logic of the entire QMS. CP 7382.850 makes this concrete: risk management is one of the two stated goals of every FDA inspection, investigators use your risk management documentation as the roadmap throughout, and risk management failures are explicitly listed as Situation 1 OAI triggers.

From design-phase requirement to system-wide expectation

• Under the old QS Regulation, risk management was most explicitly referenced in Design Controls (§ 820.30)

• Under QMSR, ISO 13485 embeds risk-based thinking across the entire standard — Clauses 4.1, 7.1, 7.3, 7.4, 7.5, 7.6, and 8.2 all contain risk-related requirements

• Comment 19 (pages 7504–7505): FDA expects manufacturers to consider risk throughout the total product lifecycle, using risk management as an input to product realization, supplier management, production, and postmarket activities

Risk management in the inspection framework (CP 7382.850)

• The stated goal of FDA inspections includes evaluating whether “risk management and risk-based decision making are effectively used in the QMS” (Part II.1, page 15)

• Diagram 1 (page 20) places patients and users at the center of inspections, surrounded by a ring representing risk management — this is the new operating philosophy

• Investigators “review the manufacturer’s risk management documentation throughout the inspection to assist with understanding product risks and associated risk controls” (Part III.1.B, page 21)

• Risk-based Approach is a standalone element within the Management Oversight QMS Area, mapping to Clause 4.1.2(b) (Attachment A, page 73)

Risk management failures as explicit OAI triggers (Part V.1.A, pages 50–51)

• Failure to establish, implement, or maintain one or more processes for risk management in product realization

• Failure to use feedback or postmarket surveillance as inputs into risk management for monitoring and maintaining product realization or improvement processes

• Failure to control design and development, including not adequately evaluating changes for risk and impact on products prior to implementation

• Failure to ensure processes, including changes, are adequately monitored, controlled, or evaluated for risk and impact on products prior to implementation

• Failure to adequately analyze data or utilize current risk information, resulting in decisions not to proceed with investigations or corrective actions, leading to unmitigated adverse health consequences — including from underestimated risk, outdated risk information, or inadequate risk management

Practical implications for ISO-certified companies

• Your ISO 14971 risk management file is no longer just a design-phase deliverable — it is the investigator’s primary navigation tool

• Risk-based decisions must be traceable: documented rationale for what was prioritized, what was accepted, and what was escalated

• Postmarket data (complaints, MDRs, CAPA trends) must feed back into risk management files — the compliance program explicitly flags failure to do so as an OAI trigger

• Risk management documentation must be current, not a static artifact from product launch

BOTTOM LINE

Risk management is no longer an ancillary expectation under QMSR — it is one of the two stated goals of every FDA device inspection, and deficiencies in risk management can directly drive enforcement action. If your risk management documentation is outdated, incomplete, or disconnected from postmarket reality, CP 7382.850 treats that as a serious finding.

FAQ 11

What role do on-the-job activity systems play in QMSR compliance?

KEY TAKEAWAY

ISO 13485 Clause 6.2 requires not just training, but evaluation of the effectiveness of actions taken to ensure competence. CP 7382.850 places Human Resources as an element within the Management Oversight QMS Area, meaning investigators will evaluate whether your workforce can actually demonstrate competence — not just whether they completed a training course.

The regulatory foundation: ISO 13485 Clause 6.2

• Clause 6.2(b): “Determine the necessary competence for personnel performing work affecting product quality”

• Clause 6.2(d): “Ensure that its personnel are aware of the relevance and importance of their activities and how they contribute to the achievement of the quality objectives”

• Clause 6.2(f): “Evaluate the effectiveness of the actions taken”

• This is not a training completion requirement — it is a competence verification and effectiveness evaluation requirement

Where this sits in the inspection framework (CP 7382.850)

• Human Resources is an element within the Management Oversight QMS Area, mapping to Clause 6.2 (Attachment A, page 73)

• Under Model 2 (baseline/PMA preapproval), Management Oversight elements are specifically evaluated

• Under both models, investigators evaluating any QMS Area may follow the thread into Human Resources if competence appears to be a factor in a finding

• CP 7382.850’s scope expansion mechanism means a competence gap found in one area can trigger evaluation of training and effectiveness records across the QMS

The gap between training records and demonstrated competence

• Traditional training systems produce completion records: employee X completed course Y on date Z

• Clause 6.2 requires more than this — it requires evidence that the training was effective: that the employee can actually perform the work competently

• This is the “knowing-doing gap” — the difference between passing a quiz and consistently applying requirements in daily work

• On-the-job activity systems bridge this gap by documenting real workplace behaviors that demonstrate competence

What this means for compliance documentation

• Activity-based evidence — documented demonstrations of correct behavior in actual work scenarios — is more robust than training certificates alone

• When an investigator asks “how do you verify that your operators are competent to perform this process?” — a training record shows awareness; an activity record shows capability

• This applies across all roles: production operators, QA personnel, design engineers, supervisors, and management

BOTTOM LINE

Clause 6.2 does not say “train people.” It says “ensure competence” and “evaluate effectiveness.” CP 7382.850 places this requirement within the Management Oversight QMS Area, linking workforce competence directly to leadership accountability. On-the-job activity systems provide the evidence that bridges the gap between training completion and demonstrated competence.

Key Citations: ISO 13485 Clause 6.2; CP 7382.850 Attachment A Management Oversight (p. 73), Part III.1.B scope expansion (p. 21); Comment 27 (pp. 7506–7507)

FAQ 12

How can activity-based systems help strengthen organizational quality culture?

KEY TAKEAWAY

FDA’s definition of quality culture — “behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes” — maps directly to activity-based compliance systems. CP 7382.850 carries this language into the inspection framework and makes Management Oversight a core evaluation area. Activity-based systems produce the contemporaneous behavioral evidence that demonstrates culture in action, not just culture on paper.

The regulatory alignment

• Comment 27 defines culture of quality through four elements: behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes

• CP 7382.850 footnote 10 (page 20) embeds this definition directly in the inspection framework: “FDA expects top management to ensure applicable regulatory requirements are met through integrating QMS processes and embracing a culture of quality”

• Activity-based systems address two of the four elements directly (activities and behaviors) and influence the other two (attitudes and processes) through practice and feedback

What activity-based compliance evidence looks like

• Documented workplace activities that demonstrate correct application of regulatory requirements in real scenarios

• Push-Do-Reflect cycles: employee receives a task tied to a specific regulatory requirement, performs it in their actual work environment, and reflects on the experience

• Supervisory verification that activities were completed correctly — not just that training was attended

• Records organized by role and regulatory requirement, creating traceable evidence of competence across the workforce

How this aligns with CP 7382.850’s inspection logic

• Investigators evaluating Management Oversight will look at Human Resources (Clause 6.2) for competence verification evidence

• Investigators evaluating any QMS Area will look for evidence that the people performing the work understand and can demonstrate the applicable requirements

• Activity records provide contemporaneous evidence — generated at the time of the work — rather than retroactive documentation created in preparation for an inspection

• This aligns with the CP’s emphasis on evaluating whether a QMS “actually functions as an integrated, risk-driven whole” rather than whether it merely exists on paper

The investigation-readiness dimension

• CP 7382.850 Part V.1.B (page 53) evaluates the “Manufacturer’s Commitments” including “commitment from top management” and “allocation of adequate resources to ensure the firm’s quality management system is operating in a state of control”

• Activity-based records demonstrate this commitment concretely: leadership invested in ongoing competence development, not just one-time training

• Even findings of competence gaps, when documented with corrective actions, demonstrate that the system is identifying and responding to issues — which is exactly what FDA wants to see

BOTTOM LINE

Activity-based compliance systems are not a replacement for traditional training. They are the verification of effectiveness that Clause 6.2 requires and that CP 7382.850 will evaluate. They produce the behavioral evidence that gives substance to FDA’s definition of quality culture — turning “behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes” from aspirational language into documented reality.
