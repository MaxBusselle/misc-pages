A Note on Structure

The field data revealed three distinct compliance gap types. The session is organized around them because each type points to a different kind of solution — and correctly identifying which type you're dealing with is the most consequential analytical step in designing Phase 2.

Habit Gap
Worker knows the rule, has the equipment, forgets or doesn't yet act automatically. Solved by repetition, cues, and reinforcement.
Knowledge Gap
Worker is missing a piece of information — about which PPE applies, or what the real risk is. Solved by education, not correction.
System Gap
The environment is creating barriers — wrong fit, no replacement cue, accelerated degradation. Solved by design, not coaching.
01
Habit Gap

Workers know the rules.
The automatic behavior isn't there yet.

The most prevalent gap in the data is habitual forgetting at moments of transition. Workers remove PPE appropriately during breaks; the re-equipping step on return isn't yet automatic.

What the Data Shows
  • Break and lunch transitions are the most cited violation context across all PPE types — eye protection, hearing protection, and gloves all spike at floor re-entry
  • Self-correction is nearly universal when managers prompt — workers fix the issue immediately without argument. This is the behavioral signature of a habit gap, not a resistance problem
  • Several managers report workers now self-correct when they see a manager approaching, before anything is said — the manager's presence has become a cue, but the internal habit hasn't formed independently
  • The "quick task" pattern mirrors the transition pattern: workers judge that task duration doesn't warrant re-equipping, and the automatic behavior of staying equipped doesn't yet override that judgment
  • Of 52 documented follow-up checks, more than 63% confirmed corrections were still holding later in the shift
💭
Things to Be Thinking About

Questions the data raises that require your organizational context to answer.

1
Corrections hold when a manager is present, but the habit hasn't formed independently yet. What does compliance look like on shifts or in areas with lower manager visibility? Is this data representative of the full floor, or the more actively supervised portion of it?
2
Break transitions are the highest-risk moment. Is there something about how breaks are structured or how workers re-enter the floor that creates unnecessary friction? This may be partly an environmental problem, not just a behavioral one.
3
The "quick task" logic appears across gloves, face shields, and hearing protection. Is this a pre-existing cultural pattern at TEREX? If so, has it been formally addressed before, and what happened?
4
Habits form through repetition and environmental cues, not reminders alone. What physical prompts currently exist at floor re-entry points? Is there a reason existing signage or PPE stations aren't functioning as cues?
What We Don't Know From This Data
  • Whether high-activity managers are working in high- or low-compliance areas — volume doesn't tell us where the problem is most severe
  • Whether compliance differs meaningfully on days without active manager monitoring
  • Whether any business units have already addressed the transition problem, and how
02
Knowledge Gap

Some workers are acting on
incomplete information.

Not every compliance gap is a habit failure. Some workers are making incorrect but logical decisions — about which PPE a task requires, what worn equipment has actually lost, or what the real consequence of non-compliance is over time.

What the Data Shows
  • Task-specific PPE gaps are prevalent — workers in standard safety glasses don't know goggles or face shields are also required for overhead, under-vehicle, or grinding work
  • Workers prefer worn-in gloves and don't know that comfort and cut resistance are decoupled — a glove can feel fine while having lost protective value
  • Workers wearing prescription glasses as their only eye protection may not know that a safety side-shield program exists or that Rx glasses alone don't meet PPE requirements
  • Chemical compatibility gaps are appearing — workers using cut-resistant gloves for solvent or chemical work, unaware that material handling gloves don't protect against chemical exposure
  • When managers connect a PPE requirement to a specific consequence — not just the rule — behavior changes faster and holds longer

"I asked if she knew what consequences could come about if proper PPE was not utilized during operation of that tool. She noted that a possible amputation and or binding of material could result if not fed through the saw blade evenly."

Floor manager — worker articulating the risk without being told what it was

The worker already knew the consequence — she just hadn't been asked to surface it. This pattern appeared repeatedly: consequence awareness was often present but dormant. Managers who asked the question rather than stating the answer saw more durable behavior change.

💭
Things to Be Thinking About

Knowledge gaps require different interventions than habit gaps. Your read on which specific knowledge is missing — and why — matters here.

1
Task-specific PPE gaps appear across multiple categories. Is this a training coverage issue, a communication issue, or something else? When workers start a new task type or rotate areas, what's the current process for communicating task-specific PPE requirements?
2
Multiple managers independently found that consequence framing is more effective than restating the rule. Has consequence-based communication been formally encouraged before, or is this something managers are discovering on their own?
3
Workers prefer worn gloves because they're more comfortable and don't understand that degradation is invisible. Is cut resistance degradation covered in onboarding or safety training, or is it assumed knowledge?
4
Several knowledge gaps have straightforward fixes — the Rx eyewear program, the chemical glove compatibility issue. Are there organizational reasons these haven't been addressed, or have they simply not been visible enough to prioritize until now?
What We Don't Know From This Data
  • How consistent task-specific PPE knowledge is across business units — E-ONE and AEV have different task contexts, and relevant gaps may differ by site
  • Whether JHA processes cover task-specific PPE and aren't being consulted, or whether the gap is in the JHAs themselves
  • Whether current onboarding and safety training content covers the specific gaps appearing in the field data
03
System Gap

Some of what we're seeing
can't be solved by coaching alone.

A portion of the compliance gap is environmental — wrong equipment available, replacement cycles misaligned with actual degradation, or physical spaces that create compliance friction. Manager reinforcement doesn't fix a system problem.

What the Data Shows
  • Loctite 243, in heavy use in at least one department, accelerates glove degradation faster than standard replacement schedules account for
  • End-of-shift checks found 38 worn or holed gloves, 20 scratched face shields and safety glasses, and 3 respiratory masks with cracked seals — all in active use, all previously undetected because no structured inspection mechanism existed
  • Bump cap fit is a documented issue at one site — workers with poor-fitting equipment are less likely to wear it consistently. The fix was available through EHS once the problem was surfaced
  • Correct glove sizes have been found out of stock at certain stations, forcing workers to use the wrong size or go without
  • Workers are independently creating environmental fixes — glove clips on belts, PPE stored with equipment — suggesting they recognize the friction and are working around it within their control
💭
Things to Be Thinking About

System gaps require decisions beyond what managers can control. Your perspective on operational feasibility and organizational appetite for structural change is essential.

1
The equipment condition findings were only visible because managers looked. Before this program, what was the mechanism for PPE condition inspection? Was there one, and where did it break down?
2
The Loctite 243 finding has a clear procurement implication. Is there a process for field observations like this to reach procurement? Or does that information typically stay on the floor?
3
Workers are inventing their own fixes — glove clips, co-locating PPE with tools. Are there mechanisms to scale good ideas that emerge from the floor? Have similar solutions been tried company-wide before?
4
The transition gap is partly behavioral and partly environmental. What does the physical layout at floor re-entry points look like across sites? Have PPE dispensers, mirrors, or reminder signage been tried — and if so, what was the outcome?
What We Don't Know From This Data
  • Whether equipment condition issues are concentrated in specific business units or distributed evenly across sites
  • Whether current procurement and replacement cadences have been reviewed in light of what the end-of-shift checks are finding
  • Whether the EHS team's picture of field conditions matches what managers found, or whether this data is new to them
