Eye Protection —
The Compliance Illusion

Safety glasses are the most frequently worn but most incorrectly used PPE on the floor. The equipment is present. The behavior is the gap.

Violation Sub-Types — Eye Protection (35 entries)

Scratched / damaged lenses

Key Observations
Highest Risk Moment Break & lunch transitions — glasses left in break room, not reset before returning to the floor
Hardest Violation to Catch Glasses on the forehead — worker appears compliant but eyes are unprotected
Systemic Gap Found Prescription glasses without safety side-shields; employees unaware of the company's Rx eyewear program
Task-Specific Risk Gasketed glasses required when working under vehicles or near metal shavings — standard glasses being worn instead
Degradation Finding Heavily scratched lenses reducing visibility — still being used because employees didn't flag the damage
How Managers Are Handling It — Field Stories
Creative Intervention
"As I walked by I would say 'what beautiful eyes you have.' And they would look at me and go what? And I stated — 'what are you missing for the day to protect your vision?' They would go 'oh shoot' and remove their glasses from their head to their face. Now it is a standing item daily — they tell me how beautiful their eyes are."
Julie Phillips — Floor Manager
Non-Verbal Coaching
"After the 9:00 break I observed the employee returning without safety glasses. I provided a non-verbal reminder by pointing to my own safety glasses. The employee immediately responded with 'Oh, whoops,' and corrected the issue. The follow-up reinforced the expectation without escalating the situation."
Adam Earnest — Floor Manager
Systemic Discovery
"Christian was starting the day and had on his prescription glasses that were not safety glasses. When I asked, he said safety glasses are needed — then realized he wasn't wearing them. He was provided side shields and reminded of our safety prescription eyeglass program."
Sarah Ennis — Floor Manager
Improvement Arc
"I acknowledged Dacota for demonstrating improved compliance with safety glasses requirements. In the past, Dacota had occasionally needed reminders. Recently, he has shown noticeable improvement. The group smiled at him."
Adam Earnest — Floor Manager

Hearing Protection —
The Forgotten Reset

Unlike other PPE violations, hearing protection failures are almost never defiant. They're habitual — and the gap almost always opens at the same predictable moment: after a break.

When & Why Violations Occur (23 entries)
After break / lunch (not reset)

3

3
Removed for comfort / to hear
1
The Behavioral Profile of Hearing Violations
Pattern Not refusal — habitual forgetting. Workers know the rule, have the equipment, and comply when reminded.
Critical Window Post-break transitions account for the single largest cluster of violations across all entries
Hidden Risk Partially worn — one plug in, one missing. Not visible at a glance; caught only by close proximity during a huddle (Jimbo Thompson)
Manager Self-Disclosure One manager openly admitted removing his own ear plugs during video training to hear better — and reflected that he should find a private area instead
Most Effective Motivator Connecting to permanent hearing loss — multiple managers independently found future consequence framing created the most immediate behavior change
From the Floor
Consequence Framing
"Ear plugs and the negative consequences of hearing loss in years to come without wearing them. She acknowledged me being right and put them in after the discussion."
Ashley Hart — Floor Manager
The Invisible Violation
"All had on their PPE except one individual had glasses on but I did not see an ear plug in one ear. When I told each employee thank you for coming prepared, the fourth indicated he did have his PPE — when he turned, I noticed he had one ear plug in and the other must have fallen out without him noticing. The other team members were surprised."
Jimbo Thompson — Floor Manager
Honest Self-Reflection
"Sitting at my desk I catch myself flipping my glasses on my head when working at the computer. When doing video training I take my ear plugs out to hear — I should go to a private area to do the training."
Randall Barnette — Floor Manager
Follow-Through Confirmed
"I stopped a team member who didn't have hearing protection in. Emphasized the importance. I checked after lunch and he had hearing protection in."
Dusty Elliott — Floor Manager

Equipment Condition —
The Gap Nobody Was Watching

Before this initiative, there was no structured mechanism to inspect PPE condition during shifts. Now there is. What managers are finding is significant.

Worn / holed gloves still in use
9
Workers prefer worn equipment
3

3
Masks — dry rot / cracked rubber
1
Scratched lenses / poor visibility
2
Replaced on the spot by manager
4
What This Data Means Operationally
Behavior Insight Workers prefer broken-in gloves. They don't know worn gloves have lost protective value — this is a knowledge gap, not defiance.
Chemical Finding Loctite 243, used heavily in one department, accelerates glove degradation. Replacement frequency needs to be adjusted — a procurement and scheduling implication.
Respiratory Risk 3 respirator masks found with dry rot and cracked rubber seals — actively in use before being flagged during an end-of-shift check
Visibility Hazard Heavily scratched safety lenses found protecting eyes — but creating poor visibility, and therefore a secondary hazard
Manager Response 4 documented cases of immediate on-the-spot replacement — managers are not just identifying issues, they're resolving them
The Stories Behind the Data
Procurement Implication
"I noticed that Loctite 243 will cause the protective side of the gloves to deteriorate and fall apart. My department uses Loctite 243 quite frequently — which means gloves will have to be replaced more frequently."
Donny Baxter — Floor Manager
Knowledge Gap Revealed
"Gloves were found to be worn in. Employees preferred them that way — but I educated them that they lost their protective qualities."
Tashan St. Victor — Floor Manager
Respiratory Risk Found
"What stood out most was that 3 team members' masks were cracked and the rubber was dry rot."
Sheridan Ernest — Floor Manager
Live Demonstration
"In the example with the blade, we tried cutting the glove with a new knife so everyone could see that the glove protected them from the cut — but not from the impact. That's why we should always do things with caution."
Peter Lebron — Floor Manager
