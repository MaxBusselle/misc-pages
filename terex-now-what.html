01
Habit Gap
Building the behaviors that run automatically
Workers know the rules and comply when prompted. The goal for Phase 2 is to close the gap between prompted compliance and automatic behavior — particularly at break transitions and during quick tasks.
Primary Pattern
PPE removed at breaks and not re-equipped on return; skipped during tasks judged as "quick"
What's Working
Corrections hold in 63%+ of follow-up checks; manager presence is becoming a behavioral cue
Phase 2 Goal
Shift from manager-prompted compliance to self-sustaining habit independent of manager presence
Type Action Rationale from Data Decision / Notes
Flint
New Activity — Break Transition Check
A targeted activity where the manager positions themselves at the floor re-entry point after a specific break, observing and coaching the re-equipping moment directly. Currently no Phase 1 activity targets this moment specifically.
 Break transitions are the #1 violation context across all PPE types. "Call Out PPE at Break Transitions" is the highest-volume activity in Phase 1 but addresses violations after the fact. A dedicated transition observation activity would target the habit formation window itself.
Include in Phase 2?
Flint
New Activity — Quick Task PPE Commitment
Manager selects a team member who regularly performs quick-task work (grinding, tacking, brief material handling) and works alongside them for a defined window, observing whether PPE stays on during short tasks and coaching the specific moment where the habit breaks.
 "Stay Protected During Quick Tasks" exists but focuses on the manager staying protected. A peer-coaching version focused on observing a team member's quick-task behavior would directly address the "I'll only be a second" pattern documented across gloves, face shields, and hearing protection.
Include in Phase 2?
Flint
Modify — Verify PPE Corrections Held
Extend the existing activity to include a second follow-up check on a different day — not just later in the same shift. This would begin to measure whether corrections are forming into persistent habits versus resetting each shift.
 Current data shows 63%+ same-shift durability, but we have no data on multi-day durability. This modification would generate that signal and increase the reinforcement frequency that habit formation requires.
Modify existing, or keep as-is?
Flint
New Activity — Peer Accountability Pair
Manager designates two team members as PPE accountability partners for a defined period. Each is responsible for prompting the other at transitions. Manager checks in at end of week on whether the peer relationship is functioning.
 The "peer ripple" pattern in Phase 1 — where recognizing one worker caused others nearby to self-correct — suggests peer influence is already operating. A structured version would extend the manager's reach and begin building the peer culture component that's currently emerging informally.
Include in Phase 2?
Org Action
Audit physical cues at floor re-entry points
Review what signage, PPE dispensers, or visual cues currently exist at break room exits and floor entry points across sites. Identify whether environmental prompts are absent, insufficient, or present but ineffective.
 Habit formation is supported by environmental cues, not just reminders. The transition gap may be partly structural — if re-entry points don't prompt re-equipping behavior, manager coaching is working against an environmental headwind.
Owner? Timeline?
Open Questions for This Section
  • Are there specific business units or shifts where the transition problem is most acute, and should Phase 2 activity prioritization reflect that?
  • Is a peer accountability structure culturally viable on the floor — or would workers experience it as surveillance rather than mutual support?
  • What would "the habit has formed" look like in practice? How would we know Phase 2 is working on habit gaps?
02
Knowledge Gap
Filling the specific information workers are missing
Some workers are making incorrect decisions based on incomplete information. Phase 2 responses here are about getting the right knowledge to the right people — through Flint activities, training, and direct communication.
Primary Pattern
Task-specific PPE gaps; worn gloves misunderstood as still protective; consequence awareness present but not surfaced
What's Working
Consequence framing is producing faster and more durable behavior change than rule-restatement; workers often already know the risk when asked
Phase 2 Goal
Close specific knowledge gaps through targeted activities and organizational communication; make consequence framing a consistent manager practice
Type Action Rationale from Data Decision / Notes
Flint
New Activity — Task-Specific PPE Audit
Manager selects one task type in their area that requires PPE beyond the baseline standard (overhead work, grinding, under-vehicle, chemical handling). They verify their team's understanding of what's required — and why — before the task begins, not after a violation occurs.
 Task-specific PPE gaps appear across all four PPE categories. Existing activities address violations in the moment but don't build pre-task knowledge. An anticipatory activity — before work begins — targets the knowledge gap rather than its downstream consequence.
Include in Phase 2?
Flint
Modify — Connect PPE Requirements to Safety Consequences
This activity already exists and is the 7th highest-volume in Phase 1. For Phase 2, add a version where the manager asks the worker to state the consequence themselves — rather than the manager providing it — to activate dormant consequence awareness rather than simply delivering information.
 Multiple Phase 1 entries show workers could articulate the consequence accurately when asked. Managers who asked the question rather than stating the answer saw more durable behavior change. The activity should be reoriented from "tell" to "draw out."
Modify existing, or add as a variant?
Flint
New Activity — PPE Equipment Knowledge Check
Manager selects a team member and, using a worn or near-end-of-life piece of PPE as a prop, asks them to assess whether it's still protective. The goal is to teach workers to recognize degradation themselves — not to rely on a manager to catch it.
 Workers prefer worn gloves and don't know degradation is invisible. Teaching workers to self-assess equipment condition builds the knowledge that prevents the system gap from recurring, rather than requiring end-of-shift manager checks indefinitely.
Include in Phase 2?
Org Action
Communicate the safety Rx eyewear program
Targeted communication to workers wearing prescription glasses that a safety Rx program exists and that standard Rx glasses without side-shields are not compliant PPE.
 Multiple Phase 1 entries show workers with prescription glasses unaware the program exists. This is a communication gap with a known fix that doesn't require new training — just direct, clear communication to the right audience.
EHS / Safety team to own. Confirm scope and timeline.
Org Action
Review task-specific PPE coverage in safety training
Audit current onboarding and safety training content to determine whether task-specific PPE requirements (overhead work, grinding, chemical handling, under-vehicle) are covered — and whether coverage is sufficient for workers who rotate tasks or areas.
 The task-specific PPE gap appears broadly across sites and departments. Whether this is a training content gap or an application gap (trained but not applied on the floor) determines the correct response. The audit is the necessary first step.
Owner? Does this sit with EHS, L&D, or operations?
Open Questions for This Section
  • Is consequence-based communication currently a formal part of manager training at TEREX, or is it emerging informally through this program?
  • Are JHAs (Job Hazard Analyses) accessible to workers at the task level, or do they live primarily with supervisors and EHS? This affects how task-specific knowledge reaches the floor.
  • Are there specific task types or departments where the knowledge gap is most urgent and should be prioritized first?
03
System Gap
Fixing what coaching can't reach
Some compliance failures are caused by the environment, not the worker. These actions require organizational decisions — on procurement, equipment standards, and inspection structures. Most of them sit outside the Flint program.
Primary Pattern
Degraded equipment in active use; chemical-accelerated glove wear; fit issues; no worker-facing inspection mechanism
What's Working
End-of-shift checks are finding issues that were previously invisible; managers are replacing equipment on the spot when they find it
Phase 2 Goal
Build inspection and replacement into routine operations; address procurement gaps surfaced by the data; reduce environmental friction at transition points
Type Action Rationale from Data Decision / Notes
Flint
Modify — Document PPE Damage Found During Shift
This activity exists but is low-volume (14 completions in Phase 1). For Phase 2, increase emphasis and add a documentation step — manager records what was found, what was replaced, and flags any pattern (e.g., recurring damage in a specific area or with a specific material).
 End-of-shift checks found significant degraded equipment in active use. Making documentation a structured output — not just a correction — creates a data trail that can inform procurement and EHS decisions over time.
Prioritize in Phase 2? Where does the documentation go?
Flint
New Activity — Worker-Led PPE Self-Inspection
Manager guides a team member through a structured self-inspection of their own PPE — checking gloves for integrity, lenses for visibility, respirator seals for cracking. The goal is to build the habit of self-inspection, not manager-only inspection.
 Current inspection structure relies entirely on managers. Workers who can self-identify degraded equipment reduce the load on the manager as the sole detection mechanism — and close the gap on days or shifts where manager checks don't happen.
Include in Phase 2?
Org Action
Review glove replacement frequency in Loctite 243 departments
Assess whether current replacement schedules for cut-resistant gloves account for chemical-accelerated degradation in departments with regular Loctite 243 use. Adjust procurement and replacement cadence accordingly.
 At least one department is cycling through glove protection faster than procurement anticipates due to Loctite 243. Workers in that department are at higher risk regardless of habit or knowledge, because the equipment is degrading faster than it's being replaced.
Procurement / EHS to own. Identify affected departments first.
Org Action
Establish a worker-facing PPE replacement request process
Ensure workers have a clear, low-friction path to request PPE replacement — and know they're expected to use it when equipment degrades. Address any barriers (stocking issues, approval friction) that make requesting a replacement more effortful than continuing to use worn gear.
 Workers continue using degraded equipment partly because requesting replacement is unclear or cumbersome. Multiple entries show managers replacing items on the spot — meaning the equipment was available, but workers didn't have a self-service mechanism to access it.
Does a process currently exist? If so, is the issue awareness or access?
Org Action
Address glove sizing stocking gaps
Review cut-resistant glove inventory across sites to ensure correct sizes are consistently stocked. At least one documented case of a worker unable to get the correct size — a system gap that no amount of manager coaching can solve.
 A worker using a wrong-sized glove has reduced dexterity and potentially reduced protection. This is a procurement and inventory management issue with a straightforward fix once the scope of the problem is understood.
Procurement to review. Flag any sites with recurring stocking issues.
Open Questions for This Section
  • Is there currently a structured pathway for field observations — like the Loctite finding — to reach procurement and generate an action? If not, what would that pathway look like?
  • Are the equipment condition issues concentrated in specific sites, or distributed? The answer affects whether this is a company-wide policy change or a targeted site-level fix.
  • What is the current EHS inspection cadence for PPE condition, independent of this program? Is the end-of-shift check something that should be formalized as a permanent practice?
