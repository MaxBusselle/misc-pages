02 / 03 Where the Gaps Are

The two areas this initiative needs to move most

Across participants and managers, the same two gaps show up consistently — and they're tightly connected to each other.

Gap 1 — Documentation Completeness
FMs know what they're seeing. The system doesn't.
Documentation completeness — "My 360 activity tells the full story of what I find on visits" — is the lowest-rated area across the entire survey at 5.7. Managers independently rate documentation specificity at 5.5, the lowest of their three areas. These two numbers are measuring the same problem from different angles.
→ 24 of 45 participants (53%) rated their own documentation at 5 or below
→ Only 10 participants rated documentation at 8 or above — the smallest high-confidence group of any area
→ No manager rated their team's documentation above an 8
"My 360 activity doesn't always tell the full story — I handle things on the spot."
Common pattern from activity coaching sessions
Gap 2 — Customer Lens on Every Visit
The instinct exists. The habit doesn't.
"Every time I visit a branch, I intentionally walk it with the same judgment a first-time customer would" scores 6.0 — tied for the lowest learner score along with proactive quality. Notably, the distribution here is the widest of any question: scores span from 3 to 10, meaning the team is highly split on how consistently this happens.
→ 11 participants (24%) rated this at 4 or below — nearly one in four say this is not a consistent practice
→ 11 participants (24%) rated this at 8 or above — a vocal high-confidence group
→ The spread suggests this is more a function of individual habit than team-wide practice
"Visit my sites and see what I notice as a Citizens customer."
Vincent Parrino, FM — describing his role in the survey
One bright spot
Root cause thinking is the team's strongest area
"Whenever I find a persistent issue, I dig into why it keeps coming back" scores 6.8 — the highest of all five areas. The team has a real foundation here. The initiative can build on that instinct and connect it to the documentation and customer lens gaps.

What the divergences tell us
Overconfidence is more common than underconfidence
Of the 33 participants with matching manager ratings, 24 rated themselves higher than their manager did — an average gap of +1.4 points. Only 7 were rated higher by their manager than themselves. Three were essentially aligned.
→ Largest overconfidence gap: +6.0 — one participant rated themselves 10/10 across the board; their manager rated them 4.0
→ Most interesting underconfidence cases: Vincent Parrino (−4.0) and Brandon Allen (−2.2) both rated themselves significantly lower than their manager did — worth exploring what's behind that
→ Best-aligned pairs: Jose Graca, Jeffrey Tommer, and Donald Knight — managers and participants essentially agreed
An open question for this meeting
Do these gaps surprise you?
The manager vs. participant divergences are some of the most useful data the baseline generates — because they reveal not just skill gaps but awareness gaps. A few things worth discussing:
? Are there participants in the large overconfidence group who you'd flag as a concern going in?
? The underconfidence cases — do those match your read of those individuals?
? Which manager's ratings surprised you most — either high or low?
What to watch at the end This baseline is the starting line. When the follow-up survey arrives in weeks 11–12, we'll measure every one of these numbers again — and show the delta. The documentation and customer lens gaps are the ones most likely to move.
