01 — The Challenge

The Gap Between Knowing the Rule and Wearing the Gear

Wave 1 data confirmed what the field already suspected: most PPE gaps at REV Group aren't about knowledge of the requirement. They're about the habit — the automatic, unsupervised, every-time behavior that compliance depends on but that rules alone can't produce. Wave 2 was designed to build that.

"This is a great opportunity for you to develop the habits and some of those skills that maybe you don't have. Maybe you just need to refine them over the next 12 weeks."
Paul Antoneau · Senior Director, Health & Safety
02 — The Initiative

Build PPE Habits with Your Team

Supervisors develop as active PPE leaders across three practice areas — modeling the standard, communicating it clearly, and monitoring it throughout the shift. Structured activities progress from the most accessible to the most demanding.

i

Show what ready looks like

Use a prepared team member at the morning huddle as a live peer example — specific gear, specific task, before assignments go out.

ii

Name the expectation explicitly

Call out PPE requirements at break transitions and connect each requirement to its specific hazard — not the rule, but the injury it prevents.

iii

Correct violations in real time

Walk two work zones during active operations — correct what's missing on the spot, acknowledge what's right in the moment. Both directions matter.

iv

Recognize consistently

Publicly acknowledge team members by name with specific gear and task details — giving the whole team a concrete picture of the standard.

v

Verify the change held

Track feedback through the shift, circle back hours later, and respond specifically to what you find — turning one-time corrections into durable behavior.

05 — The Coach at Work

Meeting Each Supervisor Where They Are

Every conversation is built around what that supervisor is working on that day — their crew, their zone, their challenge. Three exchanges from the field.

Consequence Framing Coach prepares a supervisor to connect PPE requirements to specific hazards — not the rule, but the injury
Coach
Before you head out — let me sharpen the hazard connection for each pairing so you're not improvising in the moment. Bump caps under the coach: head impacts on chassis components, lacerations and concussions. Gloves when using knives: lacerations from blade contact or slip. Which of these comes up most often on your floor?
Supervisor Production
No gloves when using knives.
Coach
That's your best spot. When you see someone grab a knife without gloves: "You need cut-resistant gloves for that — a knife slip on that material will put you in the ER with a laceration before you know what happened." Specific gear, specific consequence.
Supervisor Production
He was under the assumption that since we have some exempt areas from cut gloves that we all were. I told him that wasn't the case and if he is doing anything outside the exempt task, cut gloves must be worn to prevent lacerations.
Coach
Solid — you named the requirement and connected it to the consequence. The exempt-area confusion is exactly the kind of thing that creates gaps, so clearing that up matters. Did he put the gloves on before continuing?
Supervisor Production
He thanked me for sharing the info and said he will do it from now on.
Peer Observation Supervisor uses a compliant operator as a live model — walks the violation to see it rather than hear it
Coach
When you approached the operator without the face shield, what did you say — and did you stay until they actually had it on?
Production Lead
I asked him to take a walk, and we observed the operator wearing his face shield while grinding. I said you know wearing a face shield is a standard practice and for your safety.
Coach
Good instinct taking him to see it in person. Did he put the face shield on before he went back to work? And did you acknowledge the operator who was wearing it, in the moment?
Production Lead
Yes. I asked the operator what the benefits were of wearing a face shield while grinding. He said his personal safety is his responsibility — the company has standards, but it's on him to go home the same way he left. This seemed to hit home. He asked me to get him a brand-new face shield, which I did, and he's been wearing it.
Coach
That's a strong outcome — getting him to say it himself lands harder than telling him. And getting him a new shield on the spot removed every excuse to skip it.
Loop Closed Supervisor tracks a first-time correction, circles back hours later, and delivers specific recognition when it holds
Coach
The tracking piece is what supervisors usually skip. You'll need to jot down name, PPE item, location, and whether it's a first offense or repeat. Do you have a quick way to capture that during your shift?
Production Lead
Yes, I can make the notes.
Production Lead
I had a follow-up conversation with Darlene today about keeping safety glasses over her eyes at all times. Yesterday she had temporarily removed them while working in a tight space — first time issue. I checked back a few times during the shift and safety glasses were always in proper place. I also complimented her on the correct PPE and her positive attitude with my feedback.
Coach
That's the full loop — correction, follow-up, and specific recognition. Well done. One thing worth noting: when you complimented her, did you name the specific behavior? "Darlene, glasses have been on all shift — that's exactly what I needed to see" lands harder than a general compliment.
