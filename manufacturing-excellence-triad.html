When culture of quality is your lens
A culture of quality isn't a slogan ‚Äî it's visible, measurable behavior across the entire organization.
FDA defined "culture of quality" in the QMSR Preamble as meeting regulatory requirements through "behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes." The new inspection framework (CP 7382.850) embeds this language directly, making Management Oversight a core evaluation area. If you're claiming a culture of quality, you need proof ‚Äî not just intentions.
What a real culture of quality requires
  • Scalable visibility into what's actually happening on the floor ‚Äî not just what's in the procedures binder
  • Confidence that employees are following procedures consistently, not just that they were told to during a one-time training event
  • Evidence you can show an auditor ‚Äî documented behavior, not just documented intent
  • Proactive gap identification: you're finding and fixing problems before an inspector does, not reacting every two years
  • Connection between daily work and quality objectives at every level of the organization, from operators to executives
Why this can't stand alone
  • A culture of quality without consistent standard work enforcement is aspiration without execution ‚Äî people may value quality but not know the current procedures
  • A culture of quality without regulatory compliance is a contradiction ‚Äî if you're not operating within the regulations, you don't have a culture of quality regardless of what's on the wall
  • Culture of quality is demonstrated through behavior, not declared through policy. The only way to demonstrate it is through documented activities completed in real work

When standard work is your lens
Consistently following standard procedures affects everything ‚Äî and the ramifications are hard to overstate.
Safety, profitability, efficiency, growth, revenue, cost ‚Äî every operational metric traces back to whether people are following standard procedures. Every deviation is a risk event. Every inconsistency is a crack in the system. If you're not actively enforcing standard work at scale, everything else is built on sand.
What driving standard work really means
  • Not just documenting procedures, but actively enforcing them: reiterating, checking, supporting people's ability to learn and follow them, leading by example
  • Building cross-shift consistency: different people describing the same process the same way, every time, without preparation
  • Using up-to-date expectations: ensuring current revision procedures are what people actually follow, not the version they memorized two years ago
  • Visibility into where adherence is strong and where it's not: you can't fix what you can't see, and you can't report on what you don't measure
  • Real-world practice, not just awareness: getting people to do the steps correctly in their actual work environment, not just acknowledge them in a training module
Why this can't stand alone
  • Standard work without compliance alignment means you might be driving consistency ‚Äî but toward the wrong standard or with incomplete regulatory requirements
  • Standard work without a culture of quality means there's no organizational commitment behind the enforcement ‚Äî people see it as a chore, not a priority
  • The good news: if you truly drive standard work at scale, compliance and culture of quality automatically follow. This may be the simplest way to look at the whole problem

When compliance is your lens
You can't confidently say you're in compliance if you don't actually have visibility into what's happening.
Medical device manufacturing is regulated by the FDA, and compliance is non-negotiable. But here's the uncomfortable truth: if your only visibility comes from an inspector showing up every two years and giving you corrective actions, that's not compliance ‚Äî that's reactive firefighting. The new QMSR inspection framework evaluates whether your QMS "actually functions as an integrated, risk-driven whole" rather than whether it merely exists on paper.
What real compliance demands
  • Contemporaneous evidence ‚Äî generated at the time work is performed, not documentation created in preparation for an inspection
  • Demonstrable competence: investigators will look for evidence that people performing work can demonstrate the applicable requirements, not just that they were "trained"
  • Proactive identification and response: even findings of competence gaps, when documented with corrective actions, demonstrate the system is working ‚Äî which is what FDA wants to see
  • Fast, consistent record retrieval: when investigators sense drift ‚Äî slow retrieval, inconsistent answers, mismatched records ‚Äî sampling expands from "show me one" to "show me more"
  • Investigation readiness, not just audit readiness: CP 7382.850 evaluates manufacturer commitments including allocation of adequate resources to ensure the QMS is operating in a state of control
Why this can't stand alone
  • Compliance without standard work enforcement means things fall through the cracks ‚Äî you'll get errors, mistakes, and findings on audits because procedures aren't being followed in practice
  • Compliance without a culture of quality means you're chasing minimum requirements rather than building the organizational habits that prevent problems in the first place
  • Being proactive is a fundamentally better way to drive compliance than reacting to inspector findings every two years. Proactive requires the other two pillars
