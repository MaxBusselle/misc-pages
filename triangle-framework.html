When quality is your priority
A culture of quality isn't a slogan — it's visible, measurable behavior.

FDA defined "culture of quality" in the QMSR Preamble as meeting regulatory requirements through "behaviors, attitudes, activities, and processes." The new inspection framework (CP 7382.850) embeds this language directly, making Management Oversight a core evaluation area.

If you're claiming a culture of quality, you need scalable visibility into what's really happening across the organization. You need confidence that people are actually following procedures — not just that they were told to. And you need evidence of it that you can show an auditor.

The only way to get there is through consistent standard work and regulatory compliance — they're inseparable. A culture of quality without enforcement is just aspiration.

"FDA expects medical device manufacturers, led by individuals with executive responsibilities, to embrace a culture of quality as a key component in ensuring the manufacture of safe and effective medical devices." QMSR Preamble, Comment 27 (pp. 7506–7507)

When standard work is your priority
Consistently following standard procedures affects everything.

Safety, profitability, efficiency, growth, revenue, cost — the ramifications of not following standard procedures versus following them are hard to overstate. Every deviation is a risk event. Every inconsistency is a crack in the system.

If you're not enforcing standard work — not just documenting it, but reiterating it, checking it, supporting people's ability to follow it, and leading by example — then you're not taking your SOPs seriously. Things fall through the cracks. Errors compound. Auditors find what operators missed.

Driving standard work at scale automatically builds compliance and a culture of quality. It might be the simplest way to look at the whole problem: get people doing their jobs correctly, fully, and using up-to-date expectations — and everything else follows.

"ISO 13485 Clause 6.2 requires organizations to evaluate the effectiveness of the actions taken — a training certificate just proves someone showed up. You need actual evidence that training changed how people work." ISO 13485:2016, Clause 6.2(c) — Human Resources

When compliance is your priority
You can't confidently say you're in compliance if you don't have visibility.

Medical device manufacturing is regulated by the FDA, and compliance is essential. But here's the reality: if your only visibility comes from an inspector showing up every two years and giving you corrective actions — that's not compliance. That's reactive firefighting.

The new QMSR inspection framework (CP 7382.850) evaluates whether your QMS "actually functions as an integrated, risk-driven whole" rather than whether it merely exists on paper. Investigators will look for evidence that people performing work can demonstrate the applicable requirements.

Real compliance requires proactive standard work enforcement and a genuine culture of quality. It requires contemporaneous evidence — generated at the time of work — not documentation created in preparation for an inspection.

"CP 7382.850 Part V.1.B evaluates the manufacturer's commitments including commitment from top management and allocation of adequate resources to ensure the firm's quality management system is operating in a state of control." FDA CP 7382.850, Part V.1.B (p. 53)

The single solution
Scalable, behavior-based activities completed in the flow of real work — with the visibility to prove it.
Push an activity. Complete it on the job. Reflect on what happened. Repeat at scale.
That simple mechanism drives standard work, builds real compliance, and creates a culture of quality — with auditable evidence of all three.
