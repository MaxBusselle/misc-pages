EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Every Practice moved up, every item improved.

Across the 30 learners who completed both surveys, average self-rating rose from 5.77 to 7.32 — a +1.55-point lift on the 1–10 scale (+27% relative growth). All 11 survey items registered improvement; none regressed.

The headline read

Self-rated supervisor behaviour shifted upward across all three Practices. The lift is largest in the Practice that started lowest (Set Expectations) and smallest in the Practice that started highest (Model) — a pattern consistent with a real intervention rather than uniform self-flattery. Every one of the 11 survey items moved positively; none regressed.

Set Expectations led the change

Set Expectations was the lowest-scoring Practice at baseline (5.14) and saw the largest lift — +1.74 absolute, +34% relative growth. The lowest-scoring practice having the most room to grow is consistent with what you'd hope to see from a targeted intervention. Model started highest (6.42) and moved least (+1.38 absolute, +22% relative) — ceiling effects are at play.

What this means for the program

The baseline self-vs-manager gap is informative for two reasons. First, it gives the program a real starting line — learners walked in already humble about Set Expectations, the Practice that ended up moving most. Second, it's an honest signal of self-awareness: a cohort that under-rates itself at baseline is a cohort with room to grow into accurate self-assessment.

SELF VS MANAGER · END-STATE

At follow-up, the gap flipped — but read this with care.

Only 7 learners have both a follow-up self-assessment and a follow-up manager assessment. For that small group, learners now rate themselves higher than their managers do — the inverse of baseline. This is an early signal, not proof.

Sample caveat: N=7 is too small to support strong claims. The same 7 learners' self-rating shifts ARE captured in the main matched-pair cohort (N=30) above; the unique value of this section is just the side-by-side with manager view at end-state. Three plausible explanations

1. Confidence shift. Learners now feel more capable than they're being credited for. 2. Rater turnover. Different managers may have rated at follow-up than at baseline, with their own calibration. 3. Selection. The most engaged learners are over-represented in the N=7 that came back. The honest read: any of these could be true, and the sample is too small to separate them.

Joining the data

The LearnerID column in the self-rating files uses a different identifier scheme than in the manager files (FLiNT generates a fresh ID per survey instance). All cross-file joins in this report are done on normalised learner name (lowercased, whitespace-trimmed). 30 of 30 follow-up learners matched to their baseline records by name.

Item → Practice mapping

11 of the 13 self-rating items and 8 of the 10 manager-rating items were mapped to the three program Practices (Model, Set Expectations, Monitor). Mapping was confirmed with the program owner before analysis. Two items in each survey were dropped because they measure downstream team conditions ("PPE-related safety incidents on my team are rare" and "Other supervisors on my shift are consistent with me") rather than supervisor behaviours — they don't correspond to anything a single manager does, so including them in any Practice would have misrepresented the data.

What this report does not claim

This is a pre/post comparison without a control group. We can describe the change observed in the matched cohort. We cannot attribute the change exclusively to the program — concurrent factors (other safety initiatives, seasonal change, leadership turnover) could contribute. Self-rating shifts also include a confidence component that follows training; the manager-rating data is included to triangulate, but with much smaller samples.

Response dropoff

Of the 81 learners who completed the baseline self-assessment, 30 (37%) also completed the follow-up. Manager-rating dropoff was steeper: 66 unique learners had baseline manager ratings, but only 9 had follow-up manager ratings. The matched-pair lens used throughout this report controls for selection bias within the cohort it covers, but cannot speak to learners who did not return for follow-up.
