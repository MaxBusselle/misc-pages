Early experiences from Fresh Eyes

A look at the first three activity completions in the practice.

May 6, 2026

The first three activities have been completed in the Fresh Eyes practice on Citizens Skills Coach. Three is not a sample size to draw adoption conclusions from, and that is not the point of this update. What is worth sharing is the content of those first sessions — what the facility managers noticed, how the activity coach worked with them in the moment, and what they said about the experience in their own words.

1

Joseph Dudar, Facility Manager

Downtown Utica·Fresh Eyes Walkthrough

Five months into the FM role, Joseph was assigned the Fresh Eyes Walkthrough — the entry-level activity in the practice, asking him to visit a branch as if he were seeing it for the first time and document at least one thing he found.

Before sending him out the door, the activity coach worked through the documentation step with him — specifically the difference between a vague 360 entry and a usable one:

Activity coach exchange
Activity coach If you spot something — say, a cove base separating at the teller counter — what would a vague vs. a good 360 entry look like to you?
Joseph Vague — “Please fix the cove base on the teller line.”
Activity coach A strong entry looks like: “Cove base separating at teller counter, roughly 6 feet — not on any open work order.” The key ingredients: what it is, where exactly, how much, and whether it’s already tracked.

Joseph returned with two findings, both logged in 360 with photos. He described the first this way:

The branch vestibule is in need of a fresh coat of paint, as there are visible scuffs and dirt that create a poor first impression for customers entering the branch. Joseph Dudar · post-walkthrough write-up

In his post-activity reflection, asked to describe the most significant thing he had been missing, Joseph wrote that the chipped paint on the lower portion of the vestibule wall was “an area that I had previously missed.”

2

Mark Marterella, Facility Manager

Sewickley·Fresh Eyes Walkthrough

Four years in the FM role with 45 locations under his management, Mark fits the profile where familiarity blindness runs highest. He was assigned the Fresh Eyes Walkthrough and chose Sewickley as his site for it.

When the activity coach asked where he typically begins a branch visit, Mark gave the honest answer — and the coach used it as the moment to reset the approach:

Activity coach exchange
Activity coach When you walk a branch normally, where do you typically start?
Mark Main lobby area.
Activity coach That makes sense — it’s the hub. For this walkthrough though, you’re going to start before you even get to the lobby — in the parking lot — and work your way in as a customer would.

The change in starting point had an immediate effect. Mark generated two work orders before he entered the building — exterior basement stairwell debris and a front entrance area that needed sweeping — and a third inside for paint touch-up work. All three were documented in 360 with photos. His own reflection on the interior finding:

Inside we need to have some paint touch up work done. Nothing major but still it’s the little things that make a big difference. Mark Marterella · post-walkthrough debrief
3

John Scaglia, Senior Facility Manager

Orange branch·Keep Fresh Eyes During a Work Order Visit (difficulty 3.5)

John oversees CT and NY locations and had already completed the entry-level walkthrough at his Shelton office in a prior session. This time he was assigned the harder activity in the Fresh Eyes practice — Keep Fresh Eyes During a Work Order Visit — which asks the FM to apply the same observation discipline alongside a routine work order visit, and to document at least one issue unrelated to why they are there.

On a visit to the Orange branch for an unrelated purpose, John noticed inconsistent unpainted curbing in the drive-through. The on-site facility manager entered the work order and attached a photo. The activity coach pushed past the surface acknowledgment to ask exactly how the photo was handled, and used the answer to raise the standard:

Activity coach exchange
John Attached to the work order by the facility manager.
Activity coach Perfect — that’s exactly how it should be done. One small note: ideally you attach the photo directly rather than relying on the FM to do it. Keeps your documentation complete without the handoff. Worth owning that step yourself next time.

In his own outcome statement, John recorded that he had “completed the work order and documented at least one finding unrelated to it.” The session is worth noting on two counts: the practice working at a more senior level of the organization, and a coaching moment that quietly raised the documentation standard for a habit that could otherwise have stayed implicit.

Looking forward

These are the first three.

Across the very first time through the practice, facility managers are surfacing things they describe as previously missed, the activity coach is shaping documentation habits in real time, and the sessions read differently than a routine branch visit. Worth watching as more activities come in.
