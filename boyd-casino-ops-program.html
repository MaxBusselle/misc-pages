How Flint works

Flint is a behavior reinforcement platform. Rather than pulling managers into a classroom, Flint delivers short activities inside the flow of a real shift — built around moments that already happen, like the floor walk, the shift-start review, and the end-of-shift report.

Working alongside each manager is a personalized coach. It responds in the moment: it reads what the manager actually did, gives specific feedback, asks the sharper follow-up question, and adapts to that individual over time. The coach is what turns a one-time activity into a habit, and it is available on every shift without adding a person to the floor.

Change is measured, not assumed. Every program opens with a baseline of real behavior and closes against a follow-up, so improvement is observable.

The Program

Three development areas

Each area is grounded in what Boyd's own stakeholders identified as the highest-leverage opportunities to improve in the Casino Ops manager role.

1

In-the-Moment Coaching

The Gap

Casino Ops leaders sometimes move past team member behaviors without addressing them. Root-cause conversations and documented follow-ups are inconsistent across shifts and properties.

Why It Matters

Consistent coaching directly affects retention and performance. When leaders address behaviors in the moment, team members feel seen and held to a standard. When they don't, small issues compound. At 38% annual turnover, the cost of underdeveloped teams is already visible in the business.

What Activities Look Like

Activities are built around the floor walk, the pit walk, and shift check-ins. A manager might be asked to notice a specific behavior, deliver a direct comment about it, and reflect on how it landed. Later activities introduce structured conversation techniques, and eventually close the loop by requiring documented follow-up in WorkDay across shifts.

2

Data-Driven Floor Decisions

The Gap

Managers have access to rich operational data — the Boyd Recommendation Engine, THEO, hands per hour, labor status, utilization rates — but don't always translate it into real-time floor decisions. Games are sometimes left in suboptimal configurations, and labor adjustments can lag behind what the data already suggests.

Why It Matters

Better yielding decisions and labor calls have a direct, measurable impact on revenue and expense. Small improvements in how managers respond to the data compound across 28 properties and hundreds of shifts per week.

What Activities Look Like

Activities are anchored to the shift-start review, when a manager is already looking at the floor setup, the BRE, and labor status. Early activities ask a manager to identify one data signal and name one adjustment they made because of it. Later activities connect multiple signals into a single reasoned decision, and eventually ask managers to compare what they expected against what actually happened.

3

Upward Communication

The Gap

Shift reports and executive communications are sometimes late, incomplete, or limited to recapping events rather than surfacing insight. Leaders occasionally wait to be asked rather than proactively flagging developing situations.

Why It Matters

Executives and GMs depend on shift intelligence to make timely decisions. When that communication is delayed or thin, the window to respond narrows. Across multiple properties and markets, the quality of information flowing up the chain is a structural advantage or liability.

What Activities Look Like

Activities are built around the end-of-shift report and executive handoff. Early activities treat on-time, complete reporting as the baseline standard. Later activities ask managers to add a specific observation and a recommendation — not just what happened, but what it means and what should be done. At the advanced level, managers practice proactively escalating a developing pattern before being asked, with data and a proposed course of action.

Mock Up · Practice & Milestone Architecture

What the program actually looks like

Each of the three practices is built as a progression. Managers start at a basic milestone and advance as the behavior takes hold. Every practice is anchored to workflow moments that already exist in the shift — nothing is added to the floor.

Practice 01

Coaching Team Members in the Moment

Workflow hooks Floor walkPit walkShift check-insTeam member interactions
Basic

Notice and name a specific behavior

Deliver a direct, brief comment — positive or corrective — tied to what you just observed. Specificity is the key: what you saw, not a general impression.

Intermediate

Open a structured coaching conversation

Name the behavior, explain its impact on the team or guest, and confirm the team member understood. This is the moment feedback becomes coaching.

Advanced

Follow up on a prior coaching

Check whether the behavior changed, document the loop in WorkDay, and close or escalate. This separates managers who coach from managers who just react.

Practice 02

Making Data-Driven Shift Decisions

Workflow hooks Shift-start floor reviewBRE recommendationsTable limit reviewLabor statusEVERI compliance
Basic

Pull one metric at shift start and act on it

State what the data says and what you are adjusting as a result: table limits, labor, or game mix. The habit of translating data into a decision before the shift begins.

Intermediate

Connect two or more signals to one decision

Link BRE, THEO, hands per hour, labor, and utilization together and explain the connection before acting. This is where pattern recognition develops.

Advanced

Review a prior decision against actual outcomes

Compare what you expected to what happened and articulate what you would do differently or the same. Closing the loop turns experience into expertise.

Practice 03

Communicating Shift Intelligence Up the Chain

Workflow hooks End-of-shift reportExecutive communicationShift handoffImpact player review
Basic

Send the shift report on time, complete

Treat on-time, complete reporting as a non-negotiable standard. This is the foundation everything else is built on.

Intermediate

Add observation, interpretation, recommendation

Not just recapping what happened, but including a judgment call and the reasoning behind it. This turns a report into intelligence.

Advanced

Proactively escalate a developing pattern

Frame the issue, cite the data, and propose a response rather than waiting to be asked. This is the behavior that gets noticed at the Director level.

A sample activity

What a manager actually experiences

Below is an illustrative activity from Practice 01, at the basic milestone. It shows the prompt a manager receives and a short slice of the coaching exchange that follows.

Activity Coaching · Notice & name
Your activity this shift

On your next floor walk, watch for one specific team member behavior worth addressing — positive or corrective. Deliver a brief, direct comment tied to exactly what you saw. Then come back and tell me what you said.

Manager
A dealer kept stepping away from her game to talk to the next table. I told her: "I need you with your table during open hands — guests notice when you step away."
Coach
That was specific and you tied it to the guest, which is exactly right. One thing to sharpen: you named the behavior and the impact, but did you confirm she understood it? A quick "does that make sense?" turns a correction into a coaching moment. How did she react?
Manager
She nodded and went back. I didn't really check.
Coach
That's the line between feedback and coaching. Naming it well is the basic move, and you nailed it. The next milestone is closing the loop so it sticks. Want to try that framing on your next walk?

Built for Boyd

The program is generated from your world

A Flint initiative is not generic content. It is built in part from Boyd's own standard operating procedures, training materials, performance standards, and the tools managers use every shift. The more the program is grounded in Boyd's real context, the sharper the coach's feedback and the more personalized each manager's activities become.

01

Operating standards & philosophy

  • The Boyd operating philosophy, in your own words — built into the program directly
  • The shift report template, plus a strong example of one done well
  • Prescribed report submission times and the escalation / distribution chain
  • The formal start-of-shift checklist or rundown
  • Internal KPI benchmarks: hands-per-hour targets, jackpot processing standards, table utilization thresholds
  • The roadmap Assistant Casino Managers are responsible for, and how it's used
02

The data & tools managers use

  • What managers see in BRE, THEO, EVERI, and labor status during a shift — screens or exports are enough, no live access needed
  • How yielding games works operationally: when a table opens or closes, what signals trigger a conversion, and what a poor yielding call looks like
  • Anything that shows the data in context, the way a manager reads it mid-shift
03

Coaching & performance language

  • Boyd's internal performance-management language: what the coaching tiers are called, how "corrective" and "developmental" differ in your system
  • Any existing coaching guides or conversation frameworks already in use, so the program builds on them rather than conflicting
04

Roles & where the need is greatest

  • Internal job descriptions for Casino Manager, Assistant Casino Manager, and Slot Technical Manager
  • Turnover broken down by role, so the program targets where the 38% hits hardest

Why this matters

Every one of these inputs becomes context the coach can draw on, and a way to tune activities to the specific reality of a Boyd floor. A manager isn't practicing coaching in the abstract — they're practicing it against Boyd's standards, Boyd's data, and Boyd's language.

None of this is a prerequisite checklist. Even a handful of these sharpens the program significantly. The goal is a program that feels like it came from inside Boyd — because it did.
