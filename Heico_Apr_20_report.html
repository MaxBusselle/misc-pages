Workplace Actions
Not assignments. Real work.
Each coaching session involves the AI coach guiding your sales reps through real workplace tasks — pulling customer data, making strategic calls, reviewing CRM records, and documenting intelligence. Across 89 coaching sessions, your reps took 163 distinct workplace actions.
What counts as a workplace action? ↓
Defining a Workplace Action
A workplace action occurs when a sales rep leaves the AI coaching conversation to perform a real task (digital or physical) then returns with new information or a report that the AI uses to guide their next action. These are not quiz answers. Each action represents real work with or for real customers.

Take a quick look at that customer’s recent order history and identify the specific budget-to-actual gap. Come back once you’ve got it.

RT
Robert Tulloch
Territory Manager, Ivaco Rolling Mills · 10 sessions, 21 workplace actions
From budget gaps to multi-year roadmaps.
Questioned budget reality. Named a specific gap — orders running below budget — and uncovered three distinct business factors: a production line down for a week, new business ramp delayed, and fiscal year-end inventory drawdown.
Predicted the recovery. Called it: April shipments would be 600 tons, up 200 from budget. Then confirmed it with the customer as the factors resolved.
Built a multi-year projection. Used 3 years of historical data to draft a tonnage roadmap (5,300 tons in 2026, +5% annually). Presented it to the customer. Got honest pushback on tariff uncertainty.
Validated with conditions. Customer confirmed the growth assumption is reasonable — contingent on tariff relief. Revised 2027 to 5,500 tons. Fuel costs and political uncertainty flagged as headwinds.
Explored equipment at multiple accounts. One customer: no capex plans but watching interest rates and energy costs. Another: aging equipment with a potential new line in 2027.
The Outcome

Robert now has a documented strategic picture across multiple accounts — validated growth projections, known constraints, and trigger points for when conditions change.

ND
Noah Dorsey
Account Manager, Infasco · 10 sessions, 20 workplace actions
Turning blank CRM records into strategic footholds.
Built a foothold at Cummins from zero. No CRM notes existed. Researched the X15 engine lineup expansion on LinkedIn, called, and learned about potential increased fastener demand — gated by a market test. Set a follow-up trigger.
Turned a vague signal into specifics. All State said “we’re growing with PACCAR.” Noah drilled in: the ramp-up is real but gated by fluctuating inventory from the previous supplier.
Diagnosed a volume drop at Optimas. Orders were down. Noah investigated: internal restructuring after losing a major client — not a market issue. Different diagnosis, different response.
Led with data at General Fasteners. Brought Class 8 truck build forecasts (+30% in Q3/Q4) to the call. Customer confirmed they expect the same trend.
The Outcome

Across four accounts, Noah is systematically surfacing intelligence that enables smarter forecasting. The Cummins account went from zero CRM notes to a documented strategic opportunity with a clear timeline.

Julie Hogg
Outside Sales Rep, Sivaco · 3 sessions, 6 workplace actions
Uncovering opportunities the current setup can’t reach.
Found a $1M+ capability gap at PLP. Probed on coating weight and discovered PLP buys $1,000,000+ annually of Class B and C galvanized that Sivaco cannot supply. The galv line maxes at 1.2 min vs. the 1.4 needed for Class B.
Identified a 75-ton new market opportunity at Formedge. Met with the GM. Construction customer mandating Canadian-made products. Spec: 23mm 1018 AKCHQ (1010 fallback). Timeline: Q2–Q3 2026.
Uncovered a 20K/month conversion at Leicatex. Customer shifting from imported spool to domestic carrier wire due to import duties. Trial pending. If successful: 20,000 lbs/month starting May 2026.
The Outcome

Three coaching sessions. Three concrete business opportunities with dollar values, specifications, and timelines. Julie’s conversations show what happens when specification questions get specific enough to surface real revenue potential — and honest capability gaps.
