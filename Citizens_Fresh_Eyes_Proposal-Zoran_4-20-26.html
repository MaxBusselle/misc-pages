Program Proposal Retail Facility Managers & FM Leads Performance Program ~1 Year (3 Initiatives)
Program Overview
The challenge, the audience, and three practices.
The Challenge
FMs visit their branches regularly, but not daily. And yet even on FM visits, the same stained ceiling tile or dirty diffuser gets walked past — not because FMs don't know better, but because familiarity makes things invisible. It's a habit gap, not a knowledge gap. Quality control (QC) data confirms it: cigarette receptacles (64% pass rate), exterior windows (66%), restroom floors (74%).
The Audience
Retail Facility Managers & FM Leads
~41 frontline leaders across four eastern U.S. regions. Each manages 40–60 branches, a dispersed team of MTs and janitors, and coordinates vendor work through FAMIS 360. Reactive work dominates their time — proactive quality observation is the habit to build.
Practice 1
Fresh Eyes — Seeing What Needs Attention
Walk branches with deliberate fresh-eye awareness — see what familiarity has made invisible, document it, act on it through 360.
Initiative 1 — the pilot.
Practice 2
Building Your Team's Quality Eye
Use on-site moments and huddles to get your team noticing and flagging issues on their own — without you having to point out everything.
Initiative 2 — extends the habit to the team.
Practice 3
Keeping Vendors Accountable
Review vendor work order documentation before accepting the close — verify photos, notes, and completion quality meet standard, and follow through when they don't.
Initiative 3 — closes the vendor gap.
Why This Sequence
1. Fresh Eyes→2. Team Quality Eye→3. Vendor Accountability

Fresh Eyes — Seeing What Needs Attention — Basic

The skill
Walk a location with deliberate fresh-eye awareness — approach as a first-time visitor and document issues that have become invisible through familiarity.
When this happens in daily work
Scheduled branch visit (learner-initiated, weekly+) — the primary and most reliable hook. Reactive work order visit (on-site, daily+) — secondary, FM is already there.
What success looks like
The FM identifies and documents issues at a location that were previously being walked past. Evidence: documented findings with photos and descriptions entered into FAMIS 360.
What the FM does
Successful
FM starts from the customer entrance, works systematically, finds 3+ persisting issues with specific documentation.
Partial
FM did the walkthrough but documentation was vague — "general cleanliness" rather than specific findings with photos.
Struggle
FM reverted to their normal routine — couldn't put down the work order list and see the space as a first-time visitor would.
What happens in the field
Good
The walkthrough surfaced repetitive issues that were being walked past. Findings are specific enough to act on.
Mixed
Location was well-maintained or issues were already on open work orders — technique applied but nothing truly new surfaced.
Poor
Branch closed, FM pulled to an emergency, or visit too rushed for a deliberate scan.

Fresh Eyes — Seeing What Needs Attention — Intermediate

The skill
Identify a repetitive issue that has persisted without resolution, categorize why it has persisted, and take a specific documented action step toward resolution.
When this happens in daily work
Scheduled branch visit (weekly+) — primary. Reactive work order visit — secondary.
What success looks like
FM identifies a repetitive issue, categorizes why it persisted, and logs a specific action step with a resolution date in 360.
What the FM does
Successful
FM identifies a real repetitive issue, categorizes it correctly, and logs a specific action step with a timeline.
Partial
FM found and documented the issue but the action step is vague — no owner, no date.
Struggle
FM couldn't identify a repetitive issue — nothing came to mind, or issues felt too minor. Calibration on what "repetitive" means needs work.
What happens in the field
Good
Action step started a real resolution — vendor contacted, work order opened, timeline set.
Mixed
Action taken but resolution is unclear — vendor hasn't responded or the issue needs escalation.
Poor
External factors blocked action — vendor dispute, budget freeze, or the issue needs escalation.

Fresh Eyes — Seeing What Needs Attention — Advanced

The skill
Conduct a full quality walkthrough from the customer's perspective — rank all findings by customer impact, resolve top items immediately, build a written resolution plan for the rest, and document the work order numbers opened as a result.
When this happens in daily work
Scheduled branch visit (weekly+) or FM inspection cycle — both are natural moments for a full audit.
What success looks like
Findings list ranked by customer impact, top items resolved within 48 hours, written plan with owners and dates for the rest — all logged in 360, including the work order numbers opened as a result of the walkthrough.
What the FM does
Successful
FM walks the full customer path, ranks findings by what a customer would notice, resolves top items, builds a written plan, and documents the work order numbers opened as a result of the walkthrough.
Partial
FM did the walkthrough but prioritized by operational convenience rather than customer impact.
Struggle
FM couldn't shift out of the operations lens — kept seeing it as a maintenance problem rather than a customer experience.
What happens in the field
Good
Audit surfaced customer-visible findings, prioritization was solid, resolution is underway.
Mixed
Location was in good condition or resolution hit external barriers — vendor, budget, or branch priorities.
Poor
FM couldn't complete the full walkthrough — time pressure, branch operations, or an emergency.

Building Your Team's Quality Eye — Basic

The skill
During an on-site co-location moment, point out one quality issue to a team member and explain what "good" looks like for that specific item.
When this happens in daily work
Team member on-site co-location (several times per week).
What success looks like
Team member understands what the standard looks like and knows what to do when it falls short.
What the FM does
Successful
FM found a teachable moment, pointed out the issue, and explained the standard.
Partial
FM pointed out the issue but just said to fix it — didn't explain the standard.
Struggle
FM was on-site but didn't use the moment — stayed on task assignments only.
What happens in the field
Good
Team member engaged — asked questions or showed they could spot similar issues.
Mixed
Team member acknowledged it but seemed uncertain — hard to tell if it landed.
Poor
No co-location moment this week.

Building Your Team's Quality Eye — Intermediate

The skill
Use a team check-in or huddle to set a specific quality observation focus for the team — then follow up on what they found.
When this happens in daily work
Team huddle (weekly) — primary. On-site co-location — secondary for follow-up.
What success looks like
Team members report back observations based on the focus — evidence they're starting to look proactively.
What the FM does
Successful
FM set a clear focus, followed up, and discussed what the team found. Structured, not a passing mention.
Partial
FM set the focus but didn't follow up.
Struggle
FM couldn't identify a quality focus or defaulted to task direction.
What happens in the field
Good
Team members brought back specific observations — the focus guided them.
Mixed
Some team members engaged, others didn't.
Poor
No team check-in this week.

Building Your Team's Quality Eye — Advanced

The skill
Coach a team member through diagnosing and resolving a quality issue they identified on their own — building their judgment, not just their compliance.
When this happens in daily work
Team member on-site (several times per week) — when a team member brings a finding they found themselves.
What success looks like
Team member demonstrates judgment — understands root cause and appropriate action, not just flagging.
What the FM does
Successful
FM coached the team member through diagnosing why it persists and deciding the right path — building judgment, not just compliance.
Partial
FM jumped to the answer rather than coaching through the diagnosis.
Struggle
No team member independently identified a quality issue this week — prerequisite didn't arise.
What happens in the field
Good
Team member showed improved judgment — reasonable diagnosis, specific action plan.
Mixed
Team member's diagnosis was partially correct but missed key factors.
Poor
Team member's finding was trivial or misidentified — independent observation hasn't matured enough yet.

Keeping Vendors Accountable — Basic

The skill
Before accepting a vendor work order close, review the documentation — photos, notes, and completion quality in Lucernex or 360 — to verify the work meets standard, not just that it's marked done.
When this happens in daily work
At desk review of 360 or Lucernex — when a vendor work order is submitted for close.
What success looks like
FM reviews documentation before accepting the close — photos present, notes adequate, completion quality verified. Gaps flagged in 360 or Lucernex before the work order is accepted.
What the FM does
Successful
FM reviewed documentation before accepting — checked photos, notes, and completion quality, and flagged any gaps rather than accepting at face value.
Partial
FM noticed documentation gaps but accepted the close anyway — acknowledged it mentally, no record created.
Struggle
FM accepted the work order close without reviewing documentation — confirmed it was marked done, not whether it was done correctly.
What happens in the field
Good
Documentation gaps found and flagged before acceptance — FM caught something that would otherwise have been accepted without record.
Mixed
Documentation was complete and quality met standard — review applied correctly, nothing to flag.
Poor
No vendor work orders closed at FM locations this period.

Keeping Vendors Accountable — Intermediate

The skill
Document a pattern of vendor performance gaps across multiple locations and bring specific evidence to the next vendor check-in or Quarterly Business Review (QBR).
When this happens in daily work
360 and Lucernex review at desk — identify the pattern. Vendor check-in or Quarterly Business Review (QBR) — bring the evidence.
What success looks like
Documented pattern across locations, evidence ready for the next vendor conversation.
What the FM does
Successful
FM reviewed 360 data, identified a recurring pattern, documented examples, prepared evidence.
Partial
FM identified a pattern but relied on memory rather than 360 data.
Struggle
FM couldn't identify a cross-location pattern — not enough data or issues too scattered.
What happens in the field
Good
Evidence was compelling — vendor acknowledged the pattern and agreed to corrective action.
Mixed
Vendor pushed back or pattern wasn't clear enough to drive specific commitments.
Poor
Evidence prepared but no vendor meeting yet.

Keeping Vendors Accountable — Advanced

The skill
Use documented vendor performance data to hold a structured accountability conversation — with specific gaps, expected standards, and agreed corrective actions with timelines.
When this happens in daily work
Vendor visit or Quarterly Business Review (QBR) — the structured meeting is the natural venue. Prep via 360 review beforehand.
What success looks like
Vendor accountability conversation resulting in agreed corrective actions with timelines — documented.
What the FM does
Successful
FM held a structured conversation using documented data — specific gaps, expected standards, agreed actions with timelines.
Partial
FM raised issues but conversation was unstructured — no specific actions or timelines agreed.
Struggle
FM avoided the conversation despite having evidence — discomfort with confrontation.
What happens in the field
Good
Vendor agreed to specific actions with timelines — documented plan both parties committed to.
Mixed
Vendor acknowledged issues but commitments were vague — no specific timelines.
Poor
Vendor was defensive or dismissive — no commitments. May need escalation.

Fresh Eyes Walkthrough

The ask
On your next branch visit, walk the location as if you've never been there before — and document at least three issues you find that have been there a while.
How to do it
Before: Pick a location you visit regularly. Leave your work order list in your pocket for 15 minutes.

During: Start from the parking lot like a customer would. Work through: lobby → teller line → restrooms → back-of-house. Look at ceilings, cove bases, diffusers — the things your eye has learned to skip. Photograph each finding with a one-line description.

After: Enter each finding in 360. Three documented findings minimum.
After the activity — What did you do?
✅ I walked the location with fresh eyes and documented at least three issues I'd been walking past
⚠️ I completed the walkthrough but found fewer than three issues, or my findings were too vague to act on
❌ I wasn't able to approach the location differently from my usual visit — I kept reverting to my normal routine
After the activity — What happened?
👍 My walkthrough surfaced issues that were genuinely persisting unnoticed — the findings are specific enough to act on
🔀 I found some things but most were already known or on open work orders — not much was truly new
👎 I wasn't able to complete a full walkthrough — time pressure, branch operations, or other factors cut it short
Report back
Describe the location you walked and the most significant thing you found that you'd been missing.
What did you document and enter into 360?

Catch the Repetitive Issue

The ask
Identify one issue that has been present at a location for an extended period without being resolved — categorize why it has persisted, and take a specific, documented action step to move it toward resolution.
How to do it
Identifying: What condition have you seen multiple times that never gets resolved? Categorize it: vendor gap, deferred maintenance, or standards gap.

During visit: Document with a photo — what it is, how long it's been there, what the customer impact is. Make one specific decision about what happens next: who, what, and by when.

After: Log the issue and action step in 360.
After the activity — What did you do?
✅ I identified a repetitive issue, categorized why it persists, documented it, and took a specific logged action step toward resolution
⚠️ I identified and documented the issue, but my action step was vague or I'm not sure what the right resolution path is
❌ I had trouble identifying a repetitive issue — either nothing came to mind or the issues I found felt too minor to count
After the activity — What happened?
👍 The action step initiated a real resolution process — vendor contacted, work order opened, or repair scheduled with a date
🔀 I took action but the resolution path is unclear — waiting on a response or the timeline is uncertain
👎 External factors blocked my action — vendor dispute, budget issue, or the problem requires escalation beyond my authority
Report back
Describe the issue you found — what it is, how long it's been there, and what category it falls into (vendor, maintenance, or standards).
What specific action did you take and what's your target resolution date?

Customer-Eye Audit

The ask
Walk a location entirely from the customer's perspective — rank all findings by customer impact, resolve top items immediately, and build a written resolution plan for the rest.
How to do it
Before: Pick a location. Plan 20–30 minutes focused on the quality walk.

At location: Walk the exact customer path: parking lot → entrance → lobby → teller → ATM → restrooms → exit. Start the exterior before you step inside — check landscaping condition, signage visibility (clear of brush and readable), exterior lighting, parking lines, and curbing. For every issue, ask: would a customer notice this? Sort findings by customer impact.

After: Resolve top-priority items within 48 hours. Build a written plan for the rest — each item gets an owner and a target date. Log everything in 360, including the work order numbers opened as a result of the walkthrough.
After the activity — What did you do?
✅ I walked the location from the customer's perspective, ranked findings by customer impact, resolved top items, and built a written plan for the rest
⚠️ I completed the walkthrough and documented findings, but struggled to prioritize by customer impact — kept defaulting to operational priorities
❌ I found it difficult to separate the customer perspective from my usual FM lens — I kept defaulting to operational priorities rather than what the customer would notice
After the activity — What happened?
👍 The audit surfaced genuinely customer-visible findings and the resolution actions are underway
🔀 Location was in good condition or resolution plan hit external barriers — vendor availability, budget, or competing priorities
👎 I wasn't able to complete the full walkthrough due to time pressure or being pulled to a higher-priority issue
Report back
Walk me through the location you audited — what did you find, and what did you resolve or schedule immediately?
What's the highest customer-impact item you found, and what's the plan for it?
What work order numbers did you open as a result of this walkthrough?

Point Out One Thing

The ask

Coach Through a Finding

The ask
When a team member identifies a quality issue independently, coach them through diagnosing why it's there and deciding the right resolution path — building their judgment, not just their compliance.
How to do it
When a team member brings you a finding: Don't jump to the answer. Ask them: Why do you think this is happening? What category does it fall into? What would you do about it? Guide their thinking, then help them refine their action plan.

The Accountability Conversation

The ask
Use documented vendor performance data to hold a structured accountability conversation — with specific gaps, expected standards, and agreed corrective actions with timelines.
How to do it
Before the meeting: Organize your evidence — specific gaps, locations, dates, photos. Know the standard the work should meet.

During: Present the pattern, not individual complaints. State the expected standard. Get agreement on corrective actions with specific timelines. Document the agreements.

After: Log the agreements in 360 with follow-up dates.
