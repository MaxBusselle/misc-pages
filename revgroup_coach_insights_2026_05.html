What the coach is spending most of its time on

Across 70 completed activities, two coaching behaviors emerged with enough consistency to tell us something real about where the learning is happening.

94%
of sessions included the coach driving toward specifics — name the person, the gear, the task
74%
of sessions included the coach connecting a PPE requirement to a specific consequence or injury
63%
of sessions included pre-work — building language or pairings before the participant went out
Theme 1 — Specificity
Supervisors arrive with solid instincts — the coach makes them concrete
In nearly every session, the coach redirects from general awareness to named accountability: which area, which person, which gear. If a participant reports the team "looked good," the coach asks what tasks were active and whether the right additional PPE applied. This pattern runs across all three activities.
"The specifics are what teach the rest of the team what 'prepared' actually looks like."
Coach turn — Ryan Grove session
Theme 2 — Consequence Language
The coach builds the "why" before participants go out — not after
In 74% of sessions the coach connects requirements to specific injuries before the participant acts — and often builds the exact language they'll use. Bump cap + laceration. Fall harness + falling off the unit. The goal: participants aren't improvising the "why" when standing in front of their team.
"Let's get 2-3 pairings locked in so you're not improvising the consequence in the moment."
Coach turn — Greg Hardesty session

02 / 04  ·  Participant Dynamics

How participants are engaging — and where the friction is

Participants know their floors and their teams. The friction shows up in the follow-through steps, not in understanding the standard.

What’s Working
Participants engage with real operational detail
When participants engage, they arrive with named team members, specific work zones, and PPE scenarios tied to real tasks. The coach can build on that knowledge rather than establish it — which is why many sessions move quickly from prep to floor and back.
"Keep an eye on violations but remember to look for those in compliance and recognize them as well."
Donald Mohammed, Supervisor Production
Where Friction Shows Up
70% of sessions find no violations — making recognition the harder habit
When supervisors find full compliance, the default is a visual confirm and move on. The coach consistently pushes past that: did you call someone out specifically? Did you acknowledge by name? Compliance is high — the acknowledgment habit and the loop-close discipline aren't yet automatic.
"Some people are allowed to violate safety rules by their Leads or Managers, so they don't feel the rules apply to them."
Curt Main, Supervisor Maintenance
Patterns by Role
Production Leads are the most active segment; Managers are participating too
Production Leads account for 23 of 70 sessions and tend to arrive with the most granular floor detail. Supervisor Production titles represent the broadest footprint at 35 sessions. Notably, Managers in Engineering, EHS, Inventory Control, and Production are completing activities as well — cross-level engagement beyond the frontline supervisory layer.
Engagement Depth
Participants across roles are building language they can use on the floor
The most engaged sessions show participants constructing specific pairings, naming team members by habit, and debriefing honestly when a correction didn't land cleanly. Several participants — including an Engineering Supervisor adapting the activity to his cross-functional role — used the coaching to build something they hadn't done before.

03 / 04  ·  What the Coach Is Teaching Us

What this tells us about your workforce

The coaching patterns reveal something about how this team thinks about their role in building a PPE standard — not just completing a task.

What the Data Suggests
The gap is follow-through discipline, not knowledge of the standard
Participants across every title know their PPE requirements. What the coach surfaces is the space between knowing and closing the loop: the follow-up check, the specific public callout, the named commitment made before leaving rather than a general instruction. The standard exists. The coach is building the habits that make it hold.
Peer Voice
Participants are already articulating the insight themselves
The peer tips from this cohort are unusually specific — supervisors who processed what the activity revealed, not just completed a task.
→"If you are not in charge of the area, get the Supervisor in charge involved." — Curt Main
→"Verbally explain why you're wearing the PPE — leading by example isn't enough." — Ryan Grove
→"Look for those in compliance and recognize them, not just violations." — Donald Mohammed
→"Make sure to follow up to be sure the PPE is always worn." — Darin Shoaf
The Tension the Coach Is Navigating
Confirm and move on — versus close the loop and stay until it holds
Supervisors default to a visual confirm: gear is on, move on. The coach drives past that — did you follow up 10-15 minutes later? Was the acknowledgment public? Could the team member identify the requirement themselves? The sessions reveal a team that's good at checking — and building the discipline to convert that into an expectation others feel.
"When a team member already knows the risk, it's still worth stating it — it reinforces that you know it too and that it's not just a rule, it's a real expectation."
