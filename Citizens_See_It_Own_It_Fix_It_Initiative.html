See It, Own It, Fix It

Develop facility managers' ability to consistently spot quality and maintenance issues, take personal ownership of what they find, coach their teams to do the same, and build the daily habits that turn proactive quality awareness into the cultural norm across every Citizens location.

Overview
Initiative Structure
Three practices targeting the behavioral shift from task-focused execution to proactive quality ownership — for facility managers and the teams they lead.

⚠️ The Business Challenge

Citizens REO manages hundreds of banking locations across the eastern U.S. — and the quality of those facilities directly shapes how customers, executives, and colleagues experience the bank. The challenge isn't that people don't know what good looks like. It's that over time, recurring issues become invisible. A stained ceiling tile, a dirty HVAC diffuser, a cracked window, a faded sign — these get walked past day after day because everyone is focused on their own task list. Small problems go unreported until a customer notices, or an executive visits and sees what the team has stopped seeing. Quality varies significantly across regions and locations, and the gap between "acceptable" and "excellent" is often a matter of daily attention, not major investment. This isn't a training gap — it's a habit gap. And closing it starts with the facility managers who set the tone for their teams every day.

🛠 Facility Manager Profile

Frontline leader responsible for the condition and quality of assigned Citizens banking locations
Directly manages maintenance technicians, janitors, painters, and other field staff who are on-site daily
Operates across a portfolio of branches with varying age, condition, and traffic levels
Coordinates with Senior Facility Managers, vendors (EMCOR, JCI, Grainger), and internal stakeholders
Responsible for repairs & maintenance, PM schedules, inspections, vendor oversight, and budget tracking
Balances reactive work orders with proactive quality standards — reactive often wins
Dual role in this initiative: personally modeling quality awareness AND coaching their team to do the same
Works across regions (New England North, New England South, Mid-West, PHI/NJ, NYC/CT, RI) with varying facility complexity
Practice 1

Seeing What Needs Attention

Training your eye to notice what you've stopped noticing. Conducting deliberate walkthroughs with fresh eyes, identifying issues before they're reported, and documenting what you find — moving from "I didn't see it" to "I caught it before anyone else did."

Practice 2

Coaching Your Team to See It Too

It's not enough for the FM to spot issues — the maintenance technicians, janitors, and field staff are in the buildings every day. This practice is about coaching your team to develop the same quality eye: setting expectations, walking locations together, giving feedback, and holding people accountable so that proactive quality awareness becomes a team standard, not just a manager's responsibility.

Practice 3

Building Sustainable Quality Habits

Individual catches are good. Consistent habits are better. This practice is about embedding quality awareness into routines — creating simple systems that make proactive quality work the default, tracking patterns to prevent recurring issues, and sharing what works across locations and teams so the standard rises everywhere.

Situation-Specific
Situation-Specific Context
Activities are written for any facility manager, but the quality situations you encounter vary significantly depending on context. Here are the key scenarios where these practices come to life.
🚶
Branch Walkthrough
Scheduled & Unscheduled Site Visits
Exterior approach: signage condition, landscaping, parking lot surface, lighting, window cleanliness, entryway
Interior common areas: flooring condition, ceiling tiles, HVAC diffusers, restrooms, break rooms
Customer-facing areas: lobby furniture, teller stations, ATM areas, branding elements, wayfinding signage
Back-of-house: mechanical rooms, storage areas, electrical panels, housekeeping standards
Where quality blindness shows up
Walking the same path every visit and missing issues in areas you don't typically check
Noticing a minor issue but deferring it because "it's not that bad yet"
Focusing on active work orders and missing the ambient condition of the space
👥
Team Huddles & Check-ins
Daily & Weekly Team Communication
Setting quality expectations at the start of shifts or assignments
Reviewing what MTs and janitors are seeing in the field and what they're reporting (or not reporting)
Recognizing team members who caught issues proactively
Addressing patterns: "We keep seeing the same issue at the same locations — what's going on?"
Where quality blindness shows up
Huddles focused only on task assignments, never on quality standards or observations
No feedback loop — team reports issues but never hears what happened
Assuming team members know what "good" looks like without ever showing them
📄
Vendor & Contractor Visits
EMCOR, JCI, Grainger & Others
Inspecting vendor work quality before signing off — not just completion, but standard
Walking the site with the vendor to point out issues they should have caught
Holding vendors to SLAs and documenting missed services for QBR discussions
Using the 360 report to monitor vendor performance and flag patterns
Where quality blindness shows up
Accepting "completed" work orders without verifying quality of work on-site
Not documenting missed vendor services or substandard work
Waiting for QBRs to raise issues instead of addressing them in real time
📊
Inspections & PM Schedules
Formal Quality & Compliance Checks
FM inspections: using the checklist as a floor, not a ceiling — looking beyond the form
Preventive maintenance schedule adherence and quality of PM work completed
Equipment condition assessments: HVAC, lighting, plumbing, BMS systems
Appliance inspections and asset tracking in collaboration with engineering services
Where quality blindness shows up
Treating inspections as a checkbox exercise rather than a genuine quality assessment
PM work logged as "complete" without verification that the work met standards
Inspection findings that never lead to corrective action or follow-up
⚠️
Executive & Customer Visibility
High-Stakes Moments
Executive branch visits where facility condition reflects directly on REO's reputation
Customer-facing moments: lobby first impressions, ATM areas, exterior curb appeal
New branch openings or post-renovation handoffs where standards must be impeccable
Seasonal transitions: winter salt damage, spring landscaping, HVAC seasonal changeovers
Where quality blindness shows up
Scrambling to prep for an executive visit instead of maintaining the standard daily
Issues that are visible to customers but have been normalized by staff
Post-renovation punch list items that linger because "it's close enough"
🔁
Program Rollouts & Transitions
New Standards, Equipment & Processes
New program implementations: flooring, janitorial refresh, bollard installations, awning cleanings
Uniform and equipment transitions that affect team appearance and readiness
Playbook updates and new standard operating procedures that change how quality is measured
Technology transitions: work order systems, inspection tools, reporting dashboards
Where quality blindness shows up
New standards communicated but not followed up on — assuming the memo was enough
Inconsistent adoption across team members because coaching didn't accompany the rollout
Legacy habits persisting because the new process wasn't reinforced in the field
